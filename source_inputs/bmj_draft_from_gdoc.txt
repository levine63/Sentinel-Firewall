Title: The Sentinel Firewall: A Health-System Strategy to Decouple Informal Industrialization from Lead Exposure in LMICs
Author: David I. Levine, University of California, Berkeley
________________


Summary Box
What is already known?
* Lead exposure in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs) is a massive, largely invisible crisis causing over one million premature deaths annually.
* Primary exposure routes are increasingly decentralized, stemming from informal supply chains—artisanal cookware, lead-sulfide cosmetics, and lead-glazed ceramics—that evade traditional industrial regulation.
* Maternal calcium deficiency may increase skeletal lead mobilization during pregnancy
What are the new findings?
* We propose a "Sentinel Firewall" framework that leverages existing maternal and child health (MCH) platforms to identify high-risk districts and deploy modular intervention packages.
* Our economic model assumes benefits are 79% below midpoint estimates from the literature due to imperfect targeting, imperfect implementation by the health system and imperfect adherence by households. 
* The model shows a "Swap and Safe" package (vouchers for safe cookware and calcium or for safe cosmetics) have a Benefit-Cost Ratio (BCR) between 35:1 and 56:1. Probabilistic Sensitivity Analysis (PSA) with even more conservative assumptions (e.g., cutting each benefit by up to half, reducing the estimated effect of BLL on IQ by up to half, raising the discount rate, etc.) leave the 5th percentile of Benefit-Cost Ratio near 10:1 and 16:1. Every one of 10,000 simulated runs has a BCR above unity. 
What do the new findings imply?
* Health ministries can achieve immediate and potentially substantial risk reduction by integrating "safe product" vouchers and screening into routine antenatal care and immunization visits.
* Clinical sentinel data provides the actionable intelligence required to focus environmental enforcement on specific local markets and informal artisans.
________________


Abstract
Lead exposure remains an important and under-addressed driver of global neurodevelopmental and cardiovascular inequity. In LMICs, exposure is frequently embedded in informal economies, making top-down industrial regulation ineffective. We propose a pragmatic "Sentinel" framework that integrates low-cost environmental screening into existing MCH platforms. By deploying modular "Swap and Safe" vouchers for certified cookware, safe cosmetics, and nutritional buffers, health systems can protect the next generation during the window of maximum biological vulnerability. Our economic analysis indicates that even reducing benefits by 79% relative to full potential because of imperfect targeting, delivery, and adherence, these interventions yield a BCR of over 30:1, preserving cognitive capital and generating significant lifetime productivity gains.
________________


Main Analysis
The Crisis of Informal Exposure
While high-income nations saw blood lead levels (BLLs) plummet following the phase-out of leaded gasoline, roughly one in three children in LMICs still exceed WHO safety thresholds. The failure is one of "regulatory mismatch." Modern exposure arises from ordinary household goods—artisanal aluminum pots, eye cosmetics (kohl) made largely from lead sulfide, and ceramics with bright leaded glazes. Many of these goods are produced by small-scale artisans beyond the reach of traditional inspectors. Because lead is invisible and slow-acting, caregivers rarely observe acute symptoms that would prompt avoidance.
Recent estimates attribute over one million premature deaths annually to lead exposure and place the associated economic losses—largely from diminished cognitive potential—at trillions of US dollars per year, with the burden falling overwhelmingly on low- and middle-income countries (LMICs).¹-³
Three tractable and important exposure pathways
Although the relative importance of lead exposure pathways varies across countries and regions, three common pathways illustrate a broader pattern: geographically clustered, informal consumer goods that evade regulation yet account for a large share of exposure in specific settings.
* Artisanal aluminum cookware such as pots are commonly cast from mixed scrap, including materials containing lead. These pots can leach dangerous amounts of lead into food, particularly acidic dishes.⁵,⁶ Because these pots are used daily, even modest leaching can create high exposure.

* Lead-glazed ceramics, traditionally fired at relatively low temperature, can leach lead into food and drink. Children’s mugs and cups are of particular concern, as repeated contact with liquids results in chronic ingestion. Historic and contemporary studies consistently link lead-glazed ceramics to elevated BLLs in children.⁷

* Traditional eye cosmetics (kohl/surma) in many parts of South Asia, the Middle East, and Africa often contain galena (lead sulfide) as a primary ingredient. Use on infants and young children is common, often beginning in the neonatal period. Multiple studies document substantially higher BLLs among children (largely daughters) exposed to lead-containing kohl.⁸,⁹ Where kohl contains lead, it can lead to very high exposure because of often daily use. Moreover, it is often put on mothers, infants and young children during critical windows of the child’s neurodevelopment.
Pregnancy as a window of vulnerability and opportunity
Lead exposure during pregnancy poses particular risks.  Lead stored in maternal bone is mobilized during pregnancy and lactation, and readily crosses the placenta (and, to a much lesser extent, enters breast milk).¹⁰ Nutritional status modifies this risk: iron deficiency increases gastrointestinal absorption of lead, and inadequate calcium intake increases skeletal lead mobilization.¹¹,¹² Lead exposure also exacerbates iron-deficiency anemia, a central concern during antenatal care in LMICs.¹³
Recognizing these interactions (as well as calcium's role in reducing preeclampsia), WHO recommends iron and calcium supplementation as part of routine antenatal care (ANC).¹⁴ However, stockouts and low adherence often limit coverage, with most women consuming substantially fewer tablets than recommended.
The "Sentinel" Surveillance Framework
We argue that the health system must shift from a passive observer of symptoms to an active "Sentinel" of environmental risk. To be cost-effective, interventions must be geographically targeted:
   1. Identify "Red Districts": District Environmental Health Officers (DEHOs) conduct quarterly market sweeps using handheld X-ray fluorescence (XRF) to identify contaminated goods. For example, testing a dozen or two common products in each of the high-risk categories should suffice to identify markets with high prevalence of unsafe products (e.g., having 2 or more out of 12 samples in a category contaminated should probably be sufficient to flag a market for follow-up confirmation). These thresholds are intentionally illustrative; pilot work should calibrate them against measured BLLs and the costs of false positives versus false negatives. They also visit health clinics and identify if calcium tablets are in stock to give to pregnant women. 
   1. Priority for the intervention would be based on having multiple dangerous products, especially those most closely linked to high BLL. 
   2.  At the same time, this intervention is only applicable in settings where safe substitutes are both affordable and available at scale. Fortunately, a guaranteed market should promote safe production. 
   2. Modular Deployment: When a district is flagged, maternal and child health platforms (ANC and Immunization) trigger a targeted Safe Products package.
   3. Closing the Loop: Clinical sentinel data (infant BLLs) is shared with environmental regulators to trigger source investigation and artisan upgrading.[a]  Establishing cooperation between health and environmental agencies is both a challenge and an opportunity for this approach.
The Safe Products Package
Maternal and child health systems can function as a temporary consumer firewall against lead exposure—reducing harm immediately while generating the information and incentives needed to eliminate lead from informal supply chains.  Importantly, consumer substitution is intended to buy time while building capacity to eliminate lead sources -- it is not a long-term solution. 
In districts where specific exposure pathways are prevalent, we propose a modular package of vouchers for safe substitutes. These vouchers would be delivered through platforms that already reach a high share of mothers and infants: antenatal care (ANC) visits and routine immunizations. 
ANC and immunization visits are well suited to intervention because these are crucial times when lead exposure is most harmful, new parents are particularly likely to be willing to change behavior, and ANC and immunization reach a large majority of children in almost all regions. 
Examples include:


   * Where unsafe cookware is prevalent: a voucher for a certified lead-free cooking pot distributed at the first ANC visit, paired with reinforced iron and calcium supplementation.  This concept builds on recent successful use of vouchers for other health-related goods such as ORS and chlorine [cite Hopkins team, Kremer team in Nigeria xx, xxClear Solution]. Depending on the setting, vouchers may be paper coupons, paper QR codes, or digital vouchers. In a voucher system, shopkeepers hold the inventory of approved safe products. 

   * Where lead-glazed ceramics are common:  At the 6-month immunization visit (when WHO recommends infants start to receive complementary feeding), families receive a "Safe Start" kit with a voucher for a safe (e.g., stainless-steel) cups and spoons.

   * Where lead-based kohl is common: provision of carbon-based kohl alternatives for mothers at ANC and for infants at birth and periodic immunization visits. Although the use of kohl is often rooted in religious or protective beliefs, prior studies and WHO guidance indicate that harm-reduction approaches—promoting non-lead, carbon-based alternatives that preserve the ritual meaning of kohl—are culturally feasible.8,9, 21,22  In settings where only daughters receive kohl, the infant offer can be restricted to them. 


      * The "Removal Bonus": To increase the removal of leaded hazards from the home, the voucher for safe products can be supplemented by additional goods (e.g., safe toys for the toddler) if they surrender the old, hazardous pot, eye cosmetics, etc.
In some settings, surveillance may identify other major contributors to exposure, such as traditional medicine with lead as an ingredient [23] or other cosmetics (such as sindoor hair coloring in Nepal [24]). In such settings, analogous substitute products and education may be appropriate.

To estimate the realized impact of the Sentinel Firewall interventions, we utilize a “Reality Chain” methodology that models the sequential decay of efficacy among "compliers"—households that utilize unsafe products at baseline and receive, then adopt, safe alternatives. This approach accounts for the cumulative friction of real-world service delivery in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs). We model four primary nodes of decay: (1) Service Attendance, the probability a woman reaches a health contact point; (2) Delivery Fidelity, the probability that a tracer commodity is in stock and provided; (3) Household Adherence, the probability of consistent usage; and (4) Imperfect Targeting, which reflects the reality that even within a pre-screened "Red District," lead-hazardous products are not utilized by 100% of the population.
For interventions delivered during pregnancy, such as calcium and maternal kohl, we anchor the attendance node at 82% (range: 55–95%), consistent with UNICEF (2024) benchmarks for first antenatal care (ANC-1) visits. Fidelity for these clinical commodities is calibrated at 75% (range: 40–90%), reflecting World Bank Service Delivery Indicators (SDI) for essential medicine availability in rural clinics (Geng et al., 2021; World Bank, 2024). Adherence is differentiated by commodity type: daily-dose requirements like calcium supplements are assigned a conservative 60% mode (range: 30–85%), consistent with iron-folic acid (IFA) compliance rates in high-burden regions (Kanyangarara et al., 2017), while highly-valued hardware like lead-free cooking pots are assigned a higher 70% adherence mode (range: 40–90%).  Usage of the new pot among those receiving it will presumably be close to 100%, but we assume the exclusive use of the new pot will be lower. 
For hardware interventions requiring voucher redemption (e.g., safe cookware and infant mugs), we apply a redemption multiplier of 60% (range: 30–85%), reflecting the midpoint between low-attraction commodities like chlorine and high-value durable goods like insecticide-treated bed nets (Dupas et al., 2016). Lead-safe mugs are distributed through 6-month immunization visits (mode: 80%; range: 50–95%). By utilizing the immunization schedule as a secondary distribution or "catch-up" node, the program maximizes reach to infants born outside of facilities. 
Finally, all biological benefits are scaled by a Hazard Prevalence multiplier of 70% (range: 40–80%). This represents the probability that a household targeted within a pre-screened "Red District" is currently utilizing a lead-contaminated product, an estimate anchored in Pure Earth (2024) market surveillance showing that contamination in hotspot metalware and cosmetic markets frequently exceeds 50–80%. When these links are compounded, the resulting aggregate realism factor of approximately 0.2xx to xx ensures that our economic projections reflect the "Intent-to-Treat" (ITT) impact rather than idealized clinical efficacy.
In our simulations, the government only pays product and distribution costs if a woman attends the ANC, delivery, or immunization (depending on the commodities) and if the clinic or store distributes the product.

Conclusion
Demand-side substitution via maternal and child health systems is an emergency measure, not a replacement for structural reform. It buys time for the harder work of industrial upgrading.

3. Derivation of Lifetime Earnings (PV)
We model a 48-year working life (ages 18–65 inclusive). We assume market wages [or GDP / capita * labor’s share?] are W0= $1,500 today, the real wage [or productivity?] growth rate g= 2% per year, and the social discount rate r = 3%.  Let x = (1+g)/(1+r). 
We approximate unconditional survival from birth to working year t as
S_t = p_{18} - (p_{18}-p_{65})\left(\frac{t-1}{T-1}\right)^k,
St = p18 - (p18 - p65) [(t-1)/(T-1)]^k}

To estimate the impact of switching to a safe pot mid-pregnancy, you must account for the biological "lag" in blood lead reduction and the specific timing of hypertensive and neonatal risks.
While a sustained shift eventually lowers BLL by 7 \mu g/dL, a woman switching at 4 months (approximately 16–18 weeks gestation) will only experience that full reduction during the latter half of her pregnancy.
1. Estimating the "Effective" BLL Reduction
Blood lead has a half-life of approximately 30 days. When the primary source (the pot) is removed at month 4:
• Month 5: BLL drops by roughly 50% of the delta (3.5 \mu g/dL).
• Month 6: BLL drops by roughly 75% (5.25 \mu g/dL).
• Month 7–9: BLL approaches the new equilibrium of -7 \mu g/dL.
The Result: For the purpose of neonatal outcomes, you should use a time-weighted average for the second and third trimesters. A defensible "effective reduction" for this pregnancy would be approximately 5 \mu g/dL.
2. Impact on High Blood Pressure (Gestational Hypertension)
Gestational hypertension and preeclampsia typically manifest after 20 weeks of pregnancy. Because the intervention occurs at 16 weeks, the "safe pot" is in place just before the primary clinical risk window begins.
• The Coefficient: Based on meta-analyses (Poropat et al., 2018), each 1 \mu g/dL increase in BLL is associated with a roughly 1.6% to 2.0% increase in the odds of preeclampsia.
• The Magnitude: A 5 \mu g/dL effective reduction suggests an 8% to 10% reduction in the odds of hypertensive disorders for this specific pregnancy.
3. Impact on Preterm Birth and Low Birth Weight (LBW)
Lead acts as a trigger for premature labor through oxidative stress in the placenta and interference with progesterone. Most fetal weight gain occurs in the third trimester, making the 4-month intervention timing highly effective for birth weight.
• Birth Weight: Estimates suggest a loss of roughly 7 grams per 1 \mu g/dL (Zhang et al., 2020). A 5 \mu g/dL effective reduction would lead to an estimated 35-gram increase in birth weight.
• Preterm Birth (PTB): The odds of PTB increase by approximately 2% to 3% per 1 \mu g/dL (Larsen & Sanchez-Triana, 2023). Reducing BLL by 5 \mu g/dL would decrease the probability of a preterm birth by roughly 10% to 15%.

 To capture the nonlinear relationship between blood lead levels (BLL) and cognitive outcomes, we model child BLL as the sum of a cookware-related component and other sources, BLL_i = BLL_{pot,i} + BLL_{other,i}. For households using lead-leaching cookware, we draw BLL_{pot,i} from a triangular (PERT) distribution with minimum 3, mode 7, and maximum 9 µg/dL, consistent with experimental and field evidence on leaching magnitudes. Rather than imposing a parametric variance decomposition, we calibrate the distribution of BLL_{other} empirically so that the simulated pre-intervention distribution of total BLL matches observed moments (mean, median, upper-tail percentiles, and prevalence above policy-relevant thresholds such as 5 and 8 µg/dL). We then simulate the intervention by removing the cookware-related component for the share of households that both receive and adhere to the safe substitute, and compute individual cognitive gains using a nonlinear dose–response function. Aggregating across simulated children yields estimates of mean and total IQ gains that reflect both heterogeneity in baseline exposure and the convexity of lead-related harm.


We implement a simulation framework in which baseline child blood lead is decomposed into a cookware-related component and a residual component from other sources. For households with lead-leaching cookware, we draw BLL_{pot,i} \sim \text{PERT}(3,7,9) and specify a flexible, nonnegative distribution for BLL_{other,i} (lognormal in the baseline specification). We then simulate total baseline exposure as BLL_i^{pre} = BLL_{pot,i} + BLL_{other,i}. Parameters of the BLL_{other} distribution are chosen by simulated method of moments so that the implied distribution of BLL_i^{pre} matches observed data on the mean, median, selected upper-tail percentiles (e.g., p75 or p90), and the share of children above clinically relevant thresholds (≥5 and ≥8 µg/dL). This approach avoids imposing normality and allows the model to replicate the right-skewed nature of observed BLL distributions.
The intervention is modeled as the removal of the cookware-related component for the subset of households that both receive and use the safe alternative. Let p denote the joint probability of receipt and adherence. For a fraction p of simulated children, we set post-intervention exposure to BLL_i^{post} = BLL_{other,i}; for the remainder, BLL_i^{post} = BLL_i^{pre}. We then map both pre- and post-intervention BLL to cognitive outcomes using a nonlinear dose–response function f(\cdot), and compute individual-level gains \Delta IQ_i = f(BLL_i^{post}) - f(BLL_i^{pre}). Aggregate impacts are obtained by averaging \Delta IQ_i across the simulated population and scaling by the number of affected children.
Two limitations merit emphasis. First, the decomposition BLL = BLL_{pot} + BLL_{other} is not separately identified from cross-sectional data; matching multiple moments improves realism but does not recover the true underlying sources of exposure. Second, the baseline BLL distribution may reflect multiple correlated sources (e.g., cookware, spices, dust, and informal recycling), so assuming independence between BLL_{pot} and BLL_{other} is a simplification; we therefore report sensitivity analyses allowing modest positive correlation and alternative placements of cookware-using households within the exposure distribution. These checks show that while point estimates vary, the qualitative conclusions are robust to reasonable alternative assumptions.

Journal: BMJ Global Health Manuscript Title: Economic Evaluation of the “Sentinel Firewall”: A Stochastic Benefit-Cost Analysis of Lead-Reduction Interventions in High-Burden LMIC Districts
Recommendation: Major Revision

General Appraisal
This manuscript presents a sophisticated stochastic modeling approach to evaluate a multi-modal intervention package aimed at reducing lead exposure in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs). The authors move beyond deterministic "best-guess" estimates by utilizing a Monte Carlo framework that incorporates a "Reality Chain" of implementation decay—accounting for service attendance, delivery fidelity, and household adherence.
The use of the Iman-Conover method to induce rank correlation between institutional capacity and household compliance is a commendable methodological inclusion that addresses the endogeneity often ignored in global health economic evaluations. However, the resulting Benefit-Cost Ratios (BCRs) remain exceptionally high, which warrants rigorous scrutiny regarding the underlying human capital assumptions and the monetization of secondary health gains.
________________


Major Comments
1. The IQ-Earnings Elasticity and Labor Market Friction The authors provide a secondary simulation run using a significantly lower IQ-to-earnings multiplier (approximately 0.30% per IQ point), citing Ozawa et al. (2022). While this "pessimistic" run is a necessary stress test, the primary results rely on a mode of 1.4% (Grosse and Zhou, 2021).
In many of the "Red Districts" targeted by the Sentinel Firewall, the labor market is dominated by the informal sector and subsistence agriculture. The assumption that cognitive gains translate linearly into marginal productivity—expressed as $dEarnings / dIQ$—may be overstated where structural barriers to employment exist. The authors should provide a more robust justification for why the 1.4% figure, largely derived from middle-to-high-income contexts, is applicable here, or pivot the "headline" results toward the 0.30% run to maintain an ultra-conservative stance.
2. The "Reality Chain" and Product Stacking The model assumes a 70% adherence rate for hardware interventions (e.g., lead-safe pots). While the authors acknowledge "imperfect targeting," the model appears to treat the biological benefit as linear to usage. In environmental health, "product stacking"—where households use both the safe intervention and the legacy hazardous product—often results in non-linear exposure. If a household uses a leaded pot for only 20% of its cooking, but those dishes involve high-acid ingredients (e.g., tamarind or citrus), the reduction in blood lead levels (BLL) will be significantly less than 80%. The authors should discuss the physiological implications of intermittent use of legacy hazards.
3. Generational Discounting and VSLY Dissonance The inclusion of adult cardiovascular disease (CVD) benefits for mothers and co-resident grandparents is an innovative addition to the human capital model. However, the monetization of these DALYs using a Value of a Statistical Life Year (VSLY) multiplier—calculated as $VSLY = k \times (GDP_{PPP} / LaborShare)$—introduces a different valuation logic than the Human Capital Approach (HCA) used for the children.
Averting a DALY for a 65-year-old grandparent yields a significantly lower discounted value than the child's lifetime earnings, yet it remains sensitive to the choice of the social discount rate ($r$). The authors should present a sensitivity table showing the BCR at $r = 3\%$ vs. $r = 7\%$, as the latter is the standard benchmark for many multilateral development banks.
________________


Specific Technical Issues
            * Survival-Adjusted Productivity: The integration of the survival curve $S(t)$ from age 18 to 65 is mathematically sound, but the authors should clarify the interpolation of $p_{18}$ to $p_{65}$. The formula:
$$S(t) = p_{18} - (p_{18} - p_{65}) \left[ \frac{t - 18}{65 - 18} \right]^k$$
assumes a specific mortality concentration. A sensitivity analysis on the shape parameter $k$ would be beneficial to show how the "youth bulge" in LMIC demographics affects the results.
            * Conditioning of Costs: The cost block correctly conditions variable costs on attendance ($P_{att}$) and fidelity ($P_{fid}$). However, the "Fixed Surveillance" cost of $2.50 per birth seems optimistic if the program requires continuous XRF market monitoring and regulatory enforcement. Is this cost sustained annually, or is it a one-time start-up cost?
            * Voucher Redemption vs. Logistics: For the "Lead-Safe Pot," the model applies a logistics markup. Does this markup account for the "reverse logistics" of removing old, leaded cookware from the market, or is the firewall strictly a prospective intervention?
________________


Minor Points
               * Infant Kohl Assumption: The assumption that 100% of infants in the targeted district are baseline users of kohl (Prevalence = 1.0) is aggressive. Even in high-burden regions, there is usually some heterogeneity in traditional cosmetic use.
               * Wastage: The 10% wastage buffer is appropriate for clinical supplies but may be high for durable hardware like pots.
               * Table Formatting: The "Reality Chain" multipliers should be presented in a single, clear flowchart to allow readers to visualize the "decay" from theoretical to realized benefit.
________________


Conclusion
This is a high-quality, technically rigorous paper that addresses a major gap in lead-reduction literature. If the authors can successfully defend the IQ-earnings relationship in informal markets and provide a more nuanced view of "product stacking," this work will provide a foundational framework for lead-mitigation policy.

The persistence of everyday lead exposure
Lead exposure remains one of the largest, yet most preventable, causes of neurodevelopmental harm worldwide. Recent estimates attribute over one million premature deaths annually t[b]o lead exposure and place the associated economic losses—largely from diminished cognitive potential—at trillions of US dollars per year, with the burden falling overwhelmingly on low- and middle-income countries (LMICs).¹-³
The contrast with high-income countries is stark. Following the phase-out of leaded gasoline and paint, population blood lead levels (BLLs) declined sharply.[c] In LMICs, however, progress has stalled: roughly one in three children still have BLLs above the WHO’s reference level of concern.⁴ 
The reason is not regulatory ignorance, but regulatory mismatch. Today’s dominant exposure pathways are embedded in informal, small-scale supply chains.  That is, in many settings, lead exposure arises not from large factories but from ordinary household goods produced by unregulated small artisans.[d] At the same time, lead is invisible, cumulative, and slow-acting. Thus, caregivers rarely observe an acute symptom that would prompt avoidance. 
Recent estimates attribute over one million premature deaths annually t[e]o lead exposure and place the associated economic losses—largely from diminished cognitive potential—at trillions of US dollars per year, with the burden falling overwhelmingly on low- and middle-income countries (LMICs).¹-³


Three dominant, tractable exposure pathways
Although the relative importance [f][g]of lead exposure pathways varies across countries and regions, three common pathways illustrate a broader pattern: geographically clustered, informal consumer goods that evade regulation yet account for a large share of exposure in specific settings.
               * Artisanal aluminum c[h]ookware such as pots are commonly cast from mixed scrap, including lead-containing materials. These pots can leach dangerous amounts of lead into food, particularly acidic dishes.⁵,⁶ Because these pots are used daily, even modest leaching can create high exposure.

               * Lead-glazed ceramics, traditionally fired at relatively low temperature, can leach lead into food and drink. Children’s mugs and cups are of particular concern, as repeated contact with liquids results in chronic ingestion. Historic and contemporary studies consistently link lead-glazed ceramics to elevated BLLs in children.⁷

               * Traditional eye cosmetics (kohl/surma) in many parts of South Asia, the Middle East, and Africa contain galena (lead sulfide) as a primary ingredient. Use on infants and young children is common, often beginning in the neonatal period. Multiple studies document substantially higher BLLs among children (largely daughters) exposed to lead-containing kohl.⁸,⁹ Where kohl contains lead, it can lead to very high exposure because of often daily use[i]. Moreover, it is often put on mothers, infants and young children during critical windows of the child’s neurodevelopment.[j][k]
Pregnancy as a window of vulnerability and opportunity
Lead exposure during pregnancy poses particular risks.  Lead stored in maternal bone is mobilized during pregnancy and lactation, and readily crosses the placenta (and, to a much lesser extent, enters breast milk).¹⁰ Nutritional status modifies this risk: iron deficiency increases gastrointestinal absorption of lead, and inadequate calcium intake increases skeletal lead mobilization.¹¹,¹² Lead exposure also exacerbates iron-deficiency anemia, a central concern during antenatal care in LMICs.¹³
Recognizing these interactions, WHO recommends[l] iron and calcium supplementation[m] as part of routine antenatal care (ANC).¹⁴ However, stockouts and[n] low adherence often limit coverage, with most women consuming substantially fewer tablets than recommended.

A pragmatic firewall: targeted substitution through Antenatal Care and immunization
Maternal and child health systems can function as a temporary consumer firewall[o] against lead exposure—reducing harm immediately while generating the information and incentives needed to eliminate lead from informal [p]supply chains.
In districts where specific exposure pathways are prevalent, we propose a modular package of vouchers for safe substitutes.[q] These vouchers would be delivered through platforms that already reach a high share of mothers and infants: antenatal care (ANC) visits and routine immunizations.
Examples include:


                  * Where unsafe cookware is prevalent: a voucher for a certified lead-free cooking pot distributed at the first ANC visit, paired with reinforced iron and calcium supplementation.

                  * Where lead-glazed ceramics are common: a voucher for a lead-free (e.g., stainless-steel) mug for young children at immunization visits.

                  * Where lead-based kohl is common: provision of carbon-based kohl alternatives for mothers at ANC and for infants at birth and periodic immunization visits. Although the use of kohl is often rooted in religious or protective beliefs, prior studies and WHO guidance indicate that harm-reduction approaches—promoting non-lead, carbon-based alternatives that preserve the ritual meaning of kohl—are culturally feasible.[r]8,9, 21,22


In some settings, surveillance may identify other major contributors to exposure, such as (a) dust from nearby lead-acid battery recycling or (b) traditional medicine with lead as an ingredient. In such settings, analogous substitute products and education—such as provision of simple wet-cleaning supplies[s][t][u] and/or certified lead-free medicines—may be appropriate.
Prior work suggests that even modest reductions in blood lead levels yield large lifetime economic gains. Because these benefits are large, cost-effectiveness is likely to remain high under pessimistic assumptions, including high overhead costs, imperfect targeting, low adherence, and some families using both safe and unsafe products (as we model in the Appendix). In this respect, lead-free consumer goods resemble other preventive health technologies—such as insecticide-treated bednets in high-malaria settings—where distribution through ANC is imperfect but nonetheless yields very high social returns. As with these similar interventions, our analysis assumes incremental resources to protect core clinical functions.
At the same time, we recommend integrating a rigorous impact evaluation to identify key barriers, inform course correction[v], or determine whether continuation is warranted. 
The intent is not to permanently expand the clinical mandate, but to deploy time-limited, targeted substitutions in high-risk districts while upstream controls are implemented.[w] Substitutions are deployed only where the corresponding pathway materially contributes to exposure, avoiding unnecessary costs.
Surveillance that informs both care and regulation
National blood lead surveys can identify high-risk regions but rarely identify sources. To guide targeted substitution, health systems should complement biomonitoring with environmental market surveillance. [x]Rapid sweeps using handheld X-ray fluorescence (XRF) devices can identify contaminated products and also certify safe vendors for the voucher programs. Within districts, sub-district or market-level screening using XRF and low-cost field tests has been shown to be scalable for prioritizing contaminated areas and focusing confirmatory testing and enforcement where risks are greatest.[y]¹⁸–²⁰ Of course, even if we identify products with lead, that does not prove they are the main source of elevated BLL. Given the massive harms of lead exposure, the next section shows that targeting even modest sources of lead will almost always be cost effective. 
This surveillance does more than protect consumers: it creates actionable intelligence for supplier upgrading and enforcement, aligning short-term protection with long-term elimination.
Economic rationale
This approach[z] is likely to be highly cost-effective because it targets exposure during periods of maximal vulnerability, leverages existing high-coverage health-system touchpoints, and goes beyond education to provide concrete substitutes.  Pregnancy and early infancy are also well-established “teachable moments,” [aa][ab][ac]during which caregivers are more receptive to behavior change, particularly when advice is paired with tangible support.[ad]¹⁵ The intervention also supports existing health system goals of early ANC, timely immunizations, and reducing anemia. Because lead exposure permanently undermines cognitive development, mitigating it increases the long-run returns to investments in vaccination, nutrition, and schooling rather than competing with them. 
Even under conservative assumptions—using low end estimates of intervention effects on BLL, discounting future earnings, ignoring how lower BLL lowers crime and violence, etc.[ae]—targeted substitution during pregnancy and early childhood is likely to generate lifetime economic benefits many times the program costs (Appendix). In many plausible scenarios, the resulting increase in future tax revenue alone is the same order of magnitude as the cost of the intervention.
Toward elimination
There is no safe level of lead exposure. Demand-side interventions cannot substitute for removing lead from the economy through closed-loop recycling, product reformulation, artisan upgrading,16 and consistent enforcement.³ [af][ag]A substitution program is not a substitute for structural reform; it is an emergency measure used precisely when structural reform is slow.
Transitions away from health-system–mediated substitution can be triggered by milestones, such as the certification of lead-free supply chains, sustained reductions in market-level contamination, and enforcement capacity sufficient to prevent re-entry of hazardous products. In the absence of these, emergency measures risk becoming permanent and underfunded.
In the short run, equipping mothers with safe products and knowledge at their most receptive moments can protect the next generation, while also building political and economic support for upstream action. A temporary firewall can buy the time needed to finish the job.

Cautions and Limitations
Our economic projections are subject to several important limitations that add uncertainty to our estimate of the total societal benefit.
                              * Non-Linearity: The relationship between lead exposure and cognitive loss is non-linear; the steepest IQ decrements occur at the lowest levels of exposure (e.g., the first 10 μg/ dL . Our linear approximation of benefits may underestimate the cognitive gains for children moved from low-moderate to negligible risk, while potentially overestimating gains for those remaining at very high levels. It is unclear how these nonlinearities bias our estimates. 
                              * Nature of Evidence: While the toxicity of lead is established beyond doubt, evidence for specific product-replacement interventions largely relies largely on observational cohorts, cross-sectional leaching studies, and pre-post intervention trials rather than large-scale Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs). 
                              * Omission of Non-Cognitive Harms: This analysis calculates economic benefits solely based on projected IQ-linked earnings. It excludes the significant economic and human costs of lead-induced cardiovascular disease, renal failure, and behavioral pathology (higher impulsivity and crime). Consequently, the true Return on Investment (ROI) is likely substantially higher than presented here.

Critical Review: Cost-Effectiveness Appendix
Overall: The model has a defensible structure and reasonable parameter choices, but contains several errors, internal inconsistencies, and presentation problems that would prevent publication as-is.


Errors and Internal Inconsistencies
1. The #ERROR! cell is unresolved
There is a live spreadsheet error visible in the document. This cannot appear in a published appendix under any circumstances. The LET formula using SEQUENCE is the likely culprit — this function is unavailable in older Excel versions and some Google Sheets configurations. The NPV calculation needs to be verified against the hardcoded result of $864.17 and the discrepancy explained.
2. The NPV formula appears incorrect — and the spreadsheet itself flags this
The note “use the right formula!” appears next to the $864.17 figure, acknowledging the problem. Separately, three negative values appear (-$707.24, -$87.54, -$784.37) labeled as partial calculations for truncated survival scenarios (dying at 55, 59) — but these are never reconciled with the main estimate. The survival adjustment formula at the bottom of the sheet uses a simple linear approximation that understates mortality risk mid-career. For a published model, a year-by-year NPV sum (even in a hidden helper column) would be more defensible than an approximation.
3. The discount rate is inconsistent
The district-level section uses r = 0.04, while the main CEA uses r = 0.03. These produce materially different NPV results and the discrepancy is never explained or reconciled.
4. The BLL reduction sum of 65.5 μg/dL is implausible and unexplained
A cell shows “BLL reduction 65.5” with no label or context. This appears to be an arithmetic artifact but would confuse any reader and suggests an unchecked summation error.
5. Lead tests cost is zeroed out
The district monitoring section shows “lead tests / CHW: 10, cost: $3, total: $0.” This is clearly a formula error — the product of 10 × $3 × 1,000 CHWs should contribute ~$30,000 to district costs, not zero. The monitoring cost per baby of $5 may therefore be understated.


Methodological Concerns
6. BLL reductions are additive without justification
The 15 μg/dL total for Case 1 sums independent estimates across pathways. This assumes zero overlap in exposure sources — an infant using lead kohl, eating from lead ceramics, and crawling on lead dust would not accrue fully independent BLL reductions from removing each. The note “assumes partial additivity and imperfect adherence” is insufficient; the paper should either cite a study justifying the additivity assumption or apply an explicit overlap discount (e.g., 60–70% of summed values).
7. The 20% adherence/targeting rate is the most influential parameter and is poorly justified
This single assumption drives the average population IQ gain from 7.5 points down to 1.5 points, and the benefit-cost ratio from ~45x to ~9x. Yet the justification is absent. Where does 20% come from? CHW program adherence literature? Pilot data? A sensitivity analysis across 10–40% would dramatically strengthen the model.
8. The kohl BLL reduction range (5–12 μg/dL for infant daughters) is the largest single contributor and needs its own citation
Reference [11] is cited but the range is very wide. If this is from a single observational study rather than a trial, the upper bound of 12 μg/dL should be treated with more caution.
9. The earnings-per-IQ-point estimate may not be conservative
The note asks “is this conservative?” about the 1.5% figure from Gould (2009). For rural India specifically, the relationship between IQ and formal-sector earnings may be weaker than in higher-income settings due to labor market structure. The paper should either cite an India-specific estimate or explicitly acknowledge this as a generalization.


Presentation Problems
10. Case 2 is described as “infant son” but the logic is unclear
Case 2 omits kohl for the infant (presumably because boys don’t traditionally receive surma) but retains maternal kohl and calcium. This is a reasonable real-world distinction, but the framing “infant son” is confusing — the benefits accrue to the fetus/infant via maternal exposure reduction, not directly. This needs clearer explanation.
11. The “robustness” column is undefined
A column headed “Robustness (not as conservative)” contains values of 1 for some items but is never explained. Is this a flag? A multiplier? A sensitivity scenario? It cannot appear in a published table without a legend.
12. Typos throughout
“daugther” (twice), “suplies,” “woudl,” “havae,” “socail,” “disount,” “increasee” — these need a full proofread before submission.
13. The duplicate document
Documents 2 and 3 in the submission appear to be identical. If this is intentional (e.g., two versions), the differences should be made explicit. If not, one should be removed.


What’s Working
The two-case structure (full package vs. minimal package) is genuinely useful for showing the range of program ambition. The fiscal ROI calculation is a nice addition — showing that even government cost-recovery alone approaches break-even is a strong policy argument. The parameter table format is clean and, once the errors are fixed, is publishable in structure.

The Puzzling Persistence of Lead
Lead exposure remains one of the largest causes of neurodevelopmental harm globally. An estimated 33 million disability-adjusted life years (DALYs) were lost in 2021 due to lead, along with over 1 million premature deaths, primarily via cardiovascular disease [1, 2]. This immense burden persists despite decades of knowledge that lead is toxic. While blood lead levels (BLLs) have fallen dramatically in high-income countries after the phase-out of leaded gasoline and paint, an estimated one in three children in LMICs still have BLLs above safety thresholds [3].
Why is lead still everywhere? We argue that persistence is not because solutions are unknown, but because current strategies misalign responsibility, timing, and delivery platforms. That is, the answer lies in hidden exposure channels embedded in everyday materials. Lead is invisible, accumulative, and slow-acting. A child does not spike a fever from playing with a lead-glazed mug, nor does a mother notice IQ points lost from cooking in a recycled metal pot.                               2. Concentrate Resources: Full "Swap and Safe" interventions are deployed only in these "Red" districts.  Ideally, target specific items to the channels important in that district (or, for wet washing, that community).
                              3. Bridge the Gap: The Ministry of Health should share BLL and sentinel market data with environmental regulators to trigger source investigation.
The Economic Dividend
The proposed “Sentinel” approach is economically attractive because it targets exposure reduction during periods of maximal vulnerability using platforms that already exist. This approach also relies on low-cost substitutions rather than hoping education alone can lead to sustained behavior change. The direct costs of providing safe cookware, child feeding utensils, basic wet-cleaning supplies, calcium supplementation, and—where relevant—safe kohl are modest, on the order of tens of US dollars per mother–child pair in high-burden settings. Monitoring might cost very roughly around $5 per child (see appendix for assumptions). These costs are incurred once or infrequently, while the benefits of reduced lead exposure accrue over the life course.
A large body of epidemiological and economic literature links early-life lead exposure to persistent losses in cognitive function, educational attainment, adult earnings, and increased cardiovascular risk later in life. Although the precise magnitude of benefit varies by context and exposure pathway, the direction and scale of effects are consistent across settings.
Because the proposed interventions focus on replacing items that are used daily—such as cooking pots, children’s cups, and bowls—or on eliminating high-dose sources such as kohl where lead-sulfide is the primary ingredient, they have the potential to reduce exposure substantially. Even when accounting for publication bias, imperfect targeting, administrative overhead, and incomplete uptake, conservative assumptions suggest that the lifetime economic benefits plausibly exceed program costs by several orders of magnitude (see appendix). From a fiscal perspective, downstream gains in productivity imply the present value of future tax revenues can be many times the initial expenditures.
Detailed calculations, sensitivity analyses, and underlying assumptions are presented in the appendix. These estimates are illustrative rather than predictive and are intended to guide prioritization, not to provide precise forecasts. Nonetheless, taken together, the evidence indicates that in regions with high lead exposure, these targeted interventions to reduce lead exposure during pregnancy and early childhood are likely to be among the more cost-effective investments available to health systems.
Policy Implications: Toward Elimination
There is no safe use of lead.  While these demand-side interventions are vital for immediate harm reduction the ultimate solution lies in removing lead from the economy. As Luby and coauthors argue, we must move toward a total phase-out of lead in non-essential applications and enforce strict closed-loop recycling for batteries (until safer alternatives are economically viable) [2].
Until that regulatory will and capacity exist, the health sector cannot wait. By equipping mothers with safe tools and knowledge at their most receptive moments, we can build a firewall against lead exposure for the next generation.

Protecting Pregnant Women and Young Children from Lead Exposure 
Audience: Ministries of Health & Women/Child Development; State Health Secretaries; UNICEF/WHO country teams
Author: David Levine 
And Richard Fuller, if he wants
Purpose: Propose a practical, scalable package to reduce lead exposure during pregnancy and early childhood by replacing high-risk household items at routine health touchpoints in high-risk districts.
(1100 words) 
Why lead exposure matters for maternal and child health: Lead is a potent neurotoxin with no known safe level of exposure, and harms are largest and irreversible during pregnancy and early childhood. Prenatal and early-life lead exposure is associated with:
                              * Cognitive loss (lower IQ, attention and behavioral problems)
                              * Poor school readiness and lifetime earnings losses
                              * Adverse pregnancy outcomes, including hypertension and possibly preeclampsia
                              * Anemia and impaired growth
Globally, UNICEF and partners used biomarker data and exposure modeling to estimate that hundreds of millions of children have elevated blood lead levels (BLLs), with South Asia carrying a disproportionate burden. In many low- and middle-income countries (LMICs), lead exposure is not primarily from legacy gasoline or large factories, but from local household supply chains that intersect directly with food, cosmetics, and daily living.
Major exposure channels:  A small number of preventable channels account for most meaningful exposure among pregnant women and young children:
                              1. Cookware and utensils:  Informally produced aluminum pots, pressure cookers, ladles, and children’s cups are often cast from mixed scrap metal, including lead-containing materials. Lead leaches efficiently into acidic foods (dal, tomato, tamarind), making cookware a daily exposure source.
                              2. Ceramic and glazed dishes: Low-temperature glazes sold through informal pottery supply chains may contain lead compounds that leach into food and drink, especially from small bowls and cups that contact acidic foods and liquids and are used by children..
                              3. Traditional cosmetics (kohl/surma): Kohl (a dark eyeliner) applied to pregnant women (and, in some regions, to infants), often gets its dark color from lead sulfide rather than carbon soot.
                              4. Household dust: Dust contaminated by informal metal recycling, pottery, or deteriorating leaded products is a major pathway for toddlers. Sweeping and dry dusting can spread dust contaminated with lead into the air, making the dust even more dangerous.  
These sources are highly clustered by district, market, and supplier, which makes them both dangerous and tractable.
Replace high-risk items at existing health touchpoints: We propose a targeted replacement and protection package delivered through platforms that already reach nearly all mothers and infants.  By targeted, we mean that each element of the intervention applies only when monitoring implies that channel is a major problem. 
A. Infants and young children (in districts where children's cups have high lead content)
                              * At 6-month immunization (end of exclusive breastfeeding): Provide each child a safe stainless-steel cup for water and complementary feeding.
                              * At 9-month visit (e.g., measles immunization): Provide a safe spoon and small plate/bowl, particularly in districts where contaminated utensils or ceramics are detected.
Rationale: These items directly replace high-risk cups and bowls used daily by infants, at moments when caregivers are receptive to guidance.
B. Pregnant women (in districts where a high share of pots have high lead content)
                              * At first antenatal care (ANC) visit: Provide a safe stainless-steel cooking pot (or voucher for such a pot) intended for daily staple foods (e.g., rice/dal).
                              * Calcium supplementation alongside iron–folate: WHO already recommends calcium in populations with low dietary intake to reduce adverse pregnancy outcomes, including hypertension and possibly preeclampsia. Adequate calcium also reduces lead absorption and bone lead mobilization, offering a secondary protective benefit.
Rationale: These items directly replace high-risk cooking pots exactly when lead exposure from pots is most likely to harm the fetus.  Again, ANC is another time when families are receptive to guidance, so caregivers are more likely than usual to pay attention to a message "Use this safe pot for your and your child's food."  It is worth experimenting if a trade-in program (requiring a woman to swap out an unsafe pot) increases cost-effectiveness. 
C. Kohl/surma (in districts where leaded kohl is common)
In settings where leaded kohl use is common (e.g., parts of the Mideast and South Asia):
                              * Train community health workers (CHWs) with a simple screening method (e.g., a balance scale, as leaded kohl is very dense). Give the CHW safe kohl she can swap for any leaded kohl.
                              * Provide safe kohl to pregnant women
                              * If leaded kohl is put on female infants: include safe kohl in delivery kits.
D. Safe housecleaning (in communities where dust has high lead content)
In settings where industrial sources (e.g., battery recyclers) cause high levels of lead in dust, CHWs can  teach parents the importance of wet wiping and washing, especially in areas where toddlers crawl or sleep.  Where lead levels are very high, include safe washing supplies in ANC kits. 
This approach builds on existing delivery systems:
                              * ICDS / Anganwadi Centers already distribute nutrition and counsel caregivers.
                              * ANC and immunization visits are classic “teachable moments.”
                              * WHO recommendations already support calcium supplementation.
                              * Replacing cookware may also encourage cleaner cooking practices (LPG/electric), complementing clean-cooking initiatives.
Economic Analysis: The "Swap and Safe" Dividend
Consider a "Red District" with widespread contamination across all major channels. The total cost of the full 5-intervention package (safe kohl, cup, bowl, pot voucher, cleaning kit) is approximately $28 per mother-child pair.
Based on conservative estimates from intervention trials, the potential reduction in Blood Lead Levels (BLL) is significant:
                              * Safe Kohl: ≈ 5 μg/dL averted (Sources: Gogte et al. 1991; Lynch et al. 2000) for a cost near $4.
                              * Stainless Steel Pot: ≈ 3 μg/dL averted (Source: Weidenhamer et al. 2022) for a cost near $15.
                              * Small Plate/Bowl: ≈ 2 μg/dL averted (Source: Romieu et al. 1994 - Mexico ceramic glaze interventions)  for a cost near $3..
                              * Calcium Supplementation: ≈ 1 μg/dL averted (Source: Ettinger et al. 2009 - maternal BLL reduction), for no incremental cost if already in ANC budget.
                              * Wet Washing: ≈  1 μg/dL averted (Source: Rhoads et al. 1999 - assumes partial adherence) for a cost near $5
Even taking these low-end estimates, the combined decline in BLL could reach 12 μg/dL. Given that low-level lead exposure is the most damaging to cognition, this reduction translates roughly to restoring 13 IQ points, which corresponds to an estimated 20% increase in lifetime earnings (assuming ≈ 1.5-2% income gain per IQ point).
Assuming imperfect targeting—where only 25% of the distributed supplies reach families who were actually using contaminated items—the population-level benefit is still an average improvement of 3 IQ points and 5% higher lifetime earnings per child.  
In a low-income setting (baseline annual earnings $1,285$), a 5% increase in earnings starting at age 18 and lasting 37 years has a Present Value (PV) of approximately $2,377 (assuming a 2% discount rate and 2% real wage growth)  Even with a low marginal tax rate of 10%, the government recoups approximately $238 in future tax revenue per child—nearly 8.5 times the cost of the $28 intervention.
In a "Yellow District" where only basic interventions are needed (Safe Cup + Kohl + Calcium), the cost drops to ≈$5 per child. Our assumptions imply this intervention averts 7 μg/dL in 25% of children, resulting in a conservative population-wide earnings gain of 1.25%. The present value of that increased earnings is roughly **$600**. The tax revenue alone (≈ $60) pays for the program 12 times over.

def calculate_pv_with_growth(annual_baseline, increase_percent, discount_rate, growth_rate, start_year, duration):
    pv = 0
    annual_increase = annual_baseline * increase_percent
    for t in range(start_year, start_year + duration):
        # Earnings grow each year by growth_rate, but are discounted by discount_rate
        # Formula term for year t: (Increase * (1+g)^(t-start)) / (1+r)^t
        # Wait, usually growth applies to the wage baseline from year 0.
        # Let's assume real earnings grow 2% per year from NOW.
        
        future_value = annual_increase * ((1 + growth_rate) ** t)
        present_value_year_t = future_value / ((1 + discount_rate) ** t)
        pv += present_value_year_t
    return pv

Lead exposure causes over one million premature deaths annually and costs trillions of dollars due to lost cognitive potential [1, 2], almost entirely in low and medium-income countries (LMCIs). Unfortunately, in LMICs lead typically spreads through hard-to regulate artisanal supply chains for cookware, ceramics with lead glazes, cosmetics based on lead sulfide, and informal battery recycling. The prevailing regulatory approach is failing because it is not well suited to informal industrialization. 


We propose a pragmatic surveillance framework that integrates low-cost screening and protection into existing near-universal maternal and child health platforms. By combining targeted vouchers for safe cookware, safe toddler cups, and eye cosmetics with community-led identification of "hot spots," health systems can act as a temporary consumer firewall against lead while generating the intelligence and incentives needed to eliminate lead from informal supply chains.
The Puzzling Persistence of Lead
Lead exposure remains one of the largest causes of neurodevelopmental harm globally. While blood lead levels (BLLs) fell dramatically in high-income countries following the phase-out of leaded gasoline, an estimated one in three children in LMICs still have BLLs above safety thresholds [3].


Why is lead still everywhere? The answer lies in hidden exposure channels embedded in everyday materials. Lead is invisible, accumulative, and slow-acting. A mother does not see a fever or lower IQ when a child drinks from a lead-glazed mug.  Moreover, the primary drivers of exposure are not large industrial sites but active local supply chains for small artisans [4].
                              * Artisanal Cookware: Informally produced aluminum pots are often cast from mixed scrap metal, including recycled lead. These pots leach dangerous levels of lead into acidic foods [5, 6].
                              * Ceramic Glazes: Traditional low-temperature pottery often uses lead oxide glazes [7].
                              * Cosmetics: Traditional eye cosmetics (kohl) often contain galena (lead sulfide) as their primary ingredient [11].  Kohl with lead is particularly harmful where, as in most of Bangladesh and much of north India, it is used on infants, most often for daughters .
                              * Battery recyclers often leak high levels of lead. The resulting lead dust can permeate neighborhoods [Pure Earth, 2020xx]. Traditional cleaning methods such as sweeping and dry dusting often send particles into the air, worsening exposure.


Poor diets amplify the harms of lead exposure, especially for a pregnant woman and her fetus. Having sufficient iron [cite Kwong[3]] reduces the mothers gastrointestinal absorption of lead and calcium reduces the release of lead from the mother's bone [9, 10]. Maternal lead easily crosses the placenta, so it is an important risk factor. [Bellinger 2005xx]. Due to common low levels of dietary intake in most LMICs, the WHO already recommends that ANC visits include distribution of iron, deworming (in endemic areas) and calcium [WHO 2016][4].  Unfortunately, clinics often have stockouts and women do not prioritize the supplements, leading to low uptake (cite xx). 


These sources are highly clustered by district and community, making them dangerous but tractable if identified through layered monitoring.
A Targeted Toolkit for Prevention
We propose a targeted "Safe substitutes" package delivered through platforms that already reach nearly all mothers and infants: Antenatal Care (ANC) and Immunizations. This approach treats the health system not just as a treatment center, but as a distribution node for safety. 


In districts where specific lead exposure pathways are prevalent, we propose a targeted, time-limited modular package of vouchers for substitute products delivered through existing maternal and child health platforms (Panel 1). 
                              * For pregnant women in a district with high incidence of unsafe pots, the substitute is a voucher at the first antenatal care (ANC) visit for a lead-free cooking pot, alongside reinforcement of recommended iron, deworming, and calcium supplementation. 
                              * For young children where lead-containing ceramic mugs are common, the substitute is distrbiuting a voucher for a safe stainless-steel cup at routine immunization visits. 
                              * Where lead-based kohl is common, clinics can provide carbon-based kohl at ANC for the mother and at birth and some immunization visits for the infant. 
                              * In neighborhoods with heavily contaminated household dust, ANC visits can provide training and supplies for wet cleaning that may reduce short-term exposure (while enforcement addresses upstream sources). 


Beyond substitute products, in high-risk districts ANC visits should include checks for all lead vectors and education on safe options. To reduce cultural resistance in settings where lead-based kohl is viewed as medicinal or protective, carbon-based alternatives must be positioned as preserving the protective ritual while removing harm—an approach already used successfully in other public-health substitutions.


These substitutions and education programs will run into familiar problems such as stockouts, health staff skipping educational messages, some targeted families already using safe products, and some families simultaneously using both old and new products.  Our cost-effectiveness calculations (below) account for these concerns. 


Importantly, each substitution is deployed only where the corresponding vector contributes meaningfully to exposure. National blood lead surveys (as in Bangladesh's 2025 MICS) can identify high-risk regions but offer little insight into sources. To guide targeted substitution, health systems should complement biomonitoring with environmental market surveillance. Initial targeting can occur at the district level to prioritize resources, followed by rapid sub-district and often market-level confirmation using X-ray fluorescence or low-cost field tests [19, 20]—before deployment. This multi-stage approach balances speed with precision, avoiding both blanket distribution and missed clusters.


The Economic Dividend


The proposed approach is likely to be cost-effective because 
                              * It targets reduction in lead exposure during periods of maximal vulnerability: the few months before and the first years after birth. 
                              * It ses existing near-universal touchpoints: ANC and early immunization visits.  
                              * It goes beyond exhortations and education to also provide substitute products.
                              * Pregnancy and childbirth are classic “teachable moments” when caregivers are more likely than usual to change their behavior.
                              * Reducing kohl use increases gender equity, as kohl disproportionally disadvantages daughters. 
                              * Finally, the offer of free goods promotes the important goals of universal ANC and immunization while also fighting pervasive anemia (whether from poor diets or from lead exposure). 


The direct costs of providing safe cookware, utensils, and calcium are a few tens of US dollars per mother–child pair. In the appendix we analyze costs and benefits under conservative assumptions. For example, we account for the many failure modes listed above such as by assuming only 20% of new products displace contaminated sources. We factor in monitoring costs, compensation for clinic staff, and overhead costs; we use the low end of published estimates of how our interventions affect BLL, how BLL affects IQ, and how IQ affects earnings; and we discount earnings increases that are decades in the future. We also do not include the social value of how lower BLL reduces crime and violence [cite xx].  


Even with these conservative assumptions, it is likely that this intervention can lower average blood lead levels by several μg/dL in high-risk districts. Given the vast harms of lead, even much smaller changes can improve the present value of lifetime earnings by many hundreds to thousands of dollars (Appendix). Thus, the lifetime economic benefits are likely to be many times program costs. In many plausible scenarios, the long-run fiscal returns—through higher lifetime earnings and tax contributions—are of the same order of magnitude as program costs, even before accounting for broader social benefits (Appendix)
Toward Elimination
There is no safe level of lead. While these demand-side interventions are vital for immediate harm reduction, the ultimate solution lies in removing lead from the economy through strict closed-loop recycling, industrial phase-outs, and consistent enforcement [2]. 


Fortunately, this substitution and education intervention increases demand from safe producers and grows support for anti-lead enforcement. Moreover, the same surveillance we describe empowers both efforts to help suppliers upgrade and enforcement.  


These substitutions and education programs will run into familiar problems such as stockouts, health staff skipping educational messages, some targeted families already using safe products, and some families simultaneously using both old and new products.  Our cost-effectiveness calculations (below) account for these concerns. 


Transition away from health-system–mediated substitution can be triggered by milestones, such as the certification of lead-free supply chains, sustained reductions in market-level contamination, and enforcement capacity sufficient to prevent re-entry of hazardous products. Absent such criteria, emergency measures risk becoming permanent and underfunded.


However, babies not yet born cannot wait until that regulatory capacity exists and suppliers have upgrade. By equipping mothers with safe tools and knowledge at their most receptive moments, we can build a firewall against lead exposure for the next generation.

A defensible calibration is a midpoint of 0.35 IQ points per μg/dL, with 0.25 as a conservative low value and 0.50 as a plausible upper value. The logic is that the most relevant recent LMIC synthesis estimates that a 1 natural-log-unit increase in BLL is associated with about a 0.12 standard-deviation decline in learning; mapping 1 SD to roughly 15 IQ points implies an IQ-equivalent effect of about 1.8 IQ points per log unit of BLL (https://doi.org/10.1093/wbro/lkae010).  A local linear approximation then gives dIQ/dBLL ≈ -1.8/BLL, so at a policy-relevant LMIC mean BLL of 5 μg/dL the implied slope is about −0.36 IQ points per μg/dL, which supports 0.35 as the central value; at 7–8 μg/dL the implied slope is closer to 0.23–0.26, supporting 0.25 as a conservative lower bound; and at 3.5–4 μg/dL it is about 0.45–0.51, supporting 0.50 as an upper bound.  This range is also consistent with the older cohort literature showing that the lead–cognition relationship is nonlinear and steeper at lower BLLs, rather than well described by one global linear coefficient: Lanphear’s pooled analysis concluded there was no evidence of a threshold and that the decrement per unit of lead was larger below about 7.5 μg/dL, while Canfield and Jusko likewise found meaningful intellectual impairment well below 10 μg/dL.  
I expect most of these estimates are conservative because while the epidemiological (that is, observational) literature often suffer from omitted variable bias, there is also substantial measurement error in BLL, which may lead to downward bias in the estimated effects of lead on IQ.  Furthermore, we include only the effects on IQ, not the broader effects on non-cogitive skills, crime, etc.

Preferred main specification:
We parameterize the childhood BLL-IQ relationship using a benchmark effect equal to the midpoint of the adjusted low-level estimates reported by Canfield, Lanphear, and Jusko, all of which come from prospective cohort analyses that control for major socioeconomic and home-environment confounders and all of which find meaningful adverse cognitive effects at blood lead levels below 10 μg/dL, with evidence of steeper marginal effects at lower exposures.  We use this benchmark adjusted effect as the modal value in probabilistic sensitivity analysis. To allow for residual upward bias from unobserved confounding and transportability concerns when applying these estimates outside the original study settings, we specify a bounded PERT distribution with minimum equal to 50 percent of the benchmark, mode equal to the benchmark, and maximum equal to 125 percent of the benchmark. This specification is intentionally somewhat conservative but not fully one-sided: it allows substantial downward revision relative to the cohort literature while preserving some probability that the true effect is modestly larger than the benchmark because of low-BLL nonlinearity or attenuation from exposure measurement error. 
Conservative alternative:
As a more conservative sensitivity analysis, we retain the same benchmark effect—defined as the midpoint of the adjusted low-level estimates from Canfield, Lanphear, and Jusko—but cap the upper bound at the benchmark itself. Specifically, we use a PERT distribution with minimum equal to 50 percent of the benchmark, mode equal to the benchmark, and maximum also equal to the benchmark. This places all uncertainty on the downside relative to the published adjusted cohort evidence and can therefore be interpreted as a deliberately policy-conservative specification rather than as a symmetric representation of statistical uncertainty. Because the underlying studies already adjust for maternal IQ, home environment, and related covariates, this conservative specification should be read as allowing for residual bias beyond the published adjustments, not as implying that the adjusted cohort estimates are uninformative. 
A slightly tighter version, if you want it more terse:
We define the benchmark BLL-IQ effect as the midpoint of the adjusted low-level estimates from Canfield, Lanphear, and Jusko.  In the main probabilistic sensitivity analysis, we assign this benchmark as the mode of a PERT distribution with bounds of 0.5× and 1.25× the benchmark, reflecting substantial allowance for residual upward bias while retaining limited upside for low-BLL nonlinearity and measurement-error attenuation.  In a stricter conservative analysis, we instead set the upper bound equal to the mode, yielding a one-sided PERT distribution from 0.5× to 1.0× the benchmark. 
Two small cautions on wording. “Almost symmetrically” is not quite right for 0.5 to 1.25 around a mode of 1.0, because the downside span is larger than the upside span. And “midpoint of estimates” is fine, but “midpoint of the adjusted low-level estimates” is harder to attack.

Here is the cleanest reading of the literature.
Bottom line
The strongest evidence is not “BLL directly lowers earnings” but a two-step chain: higher childhood blood lead levels (BLLs) reduce IQ, test scores, and other cognitive/non-cognitive skills; those skill losses then lower adult socioeconomic status and, plausibly, earnings. The first link is strong and replicated. The second link is also well established, including in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs). But the literature that directly estimates BLL → adult earnings, especially in LMICs, is still very thin. So for LMICs today, most earnings claims are modeled extrapolations, not direct causal estimates. 
What is firmly established
1) Childhood lead exposure lowers cognition, with no clear safe threshold
The canonical studies are Canfield et al. and the international pooled analysis by Lanphear et al. Canfield et al. found that blood lead concentrations below 10 μg/dL were still inversely associated with IQ. Lanphear et al., pooling seven prospective cohorts, concluded that the lead-IQ relationship extends below 10 μg/dL and is steeper at lower levels. 
The Mexico City cohort is especially important for LMIC-relevant exposure ranges. Schnaas et al. found that IQ at ages 6–10 fell with higher third-trimester maternal BLL; the dose-response was log-linear, and “half of the deleterious effects” in their observed range occurred as maternal BLL rose from 1 to 6 μg/dL. That is one reason the “no safe threshold” position is widely accepted. 
2) The LMIC evidence now supports meaningful learning losses
A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis focused on developing-country learning outcomes found a benchmark estimate of about −0.12 standard deviations in learning per one natural-log-unit increase in blood lead, after adjustments for observable confounding and publication bias. The authors argue this could explain over one-fifth of the rich-poor country learning gap, though that particular decomposition is model-dependent. Importantly, they are explicit that most included estimates are observational, not experimental. 
This is probably the most policy-relevant recent synthesis for LMICs because it shifts the emphasis from IQ alone to school-relevant outcomes such as reading and mathematics. 
3) Lead exposure remains disproportionately an LMIC problem
A 2021 systematic review of BLLs in LMICs concluded that many children still exceed 5 μg/dL despite the phase-out of leaded petrol. More recent burden studies likewise estimate that the overwhelming majority of global lead-attributable IQ loss occurs in LMICs. A 2023 global modeling study estimated 765 million IQ points lost among children under 5 in 2019, with about 95% of those losses in LMICs. 
Recent syntheses also stress that contemporary exposure sources in LMICs are not just legacy paint and gasoline but especially informal lead-acid battery recycling, industrial emissions, contaminated spices, ceramics, cookware, and e-waste. 
What we know about earnings
A) Direct BLL → earnings evidence exists, but mostly outside LMICs
There are a few serious long-run studies, but they are mostly from high-income settings.
Reuben et al. followed a New Zealand birth cohort and found that each 5 μg/dL higher childhood BLL was associated with a 1.79-unit lower adult socioeconomic-status score, with about 40% of downward mobility mediated by cognitive decline. That is not a wage estimate, but it is direct evidence that childhood lead exposure damages later economic position. 
Banzhaf et al. used U.S. variation from airborne lead reductions linked to the Clean Air Act and estimated that a 0.5 μg/m³ decrease in in utero air lead was associated with about a 3.5% increase in adult earnings. This is one of the cleanest direct earnings papers, but again it is U.S.-based, not LMIC-based. 
Swedish evidence from the phase-out of leaded gasoline shows improved school performance, high-school completion, and lower crime from reduced early-life exposure, especially for boys and disadvantaged children. But that paper did not find clear earnings effects at the ages observed, likely because many subjects had not fully sorted into long-run earnings yet. 
There is also quasi-experimental evidence from Chile on childhood exposure to urban lead contamination harming educational achievement, with earnings losses then inferred from the score-wage relationship rather than estimated as a direct reduced-form wage effect. That is useful, but it remains one step removed from a true BLL-to-earnings estimate. 
B) For LMICs, the main earnings evidence is indirect: cognition earns money
A 2022 systematic review of LMIC studies on cognitive ability and economic outcomes found that a 1 standard deviation increase in cognitive skill is associated with roughly 4.5% higher wages on average. That is highly relevant because it provides the second half of the “lead lowers earnings through skill loss” channel in settings closer to your policy question. 
Within that LMIC review, the estimated returns are heterogeneous but broadly positive: around 4.7% higher monthly earnings per 1 SD cognitive-skill increase in Mexico; 4.8% in pooled STEP/PIAAC countries including Ghana, Kenya, and Vietnam; 1.3% in one Chinese cohort; and larger wage effects in other Chinese samples. Ghanaian evidence also links stronger executive function to higher probabilities of white-collar employment. 
So the current LMIC case for earnings losses is:
                                                                                       1. lead reduces cognition/learning;

                                                                                       2. cognition/learning predicts wages and occupational sorting in LMICs;

                                                                                       3. therefore lead likely depresses earnings in LMICs, even though direct causal earnings studies are scarce.

That syllogism is reasonable, but it is still an extrapolation. 
LMIC-specific studies worth foregrounding
If your emphasis is “relevant for LMICs today,” I would prioritize these:
Mexico City prospective cohorts. These remain among the best non-U.S. longitudinal datasets and show both prenatal and childhood lead effects on later IQ, with exposure ranges that still look familiar in many LMICs today. 
Bangladesh. Rodrigues et al. found that where BLLs were high, lead was associated with lower neurodevelopmental scores in 2- to 3-year-olds, even in a setting with concurrent arsenic and manganese exposure. This matters because contemporary LMIC exposure is often multi-toxicant, not “lead only.” 
South Africa. The Birth to Twenty Plus cohort linked elevated adolescent BLLs to later violent behavior. This is not an earnings paper, but it is economically relevant because labor-market effects of lead likely operate partly through non-cognitive and behavioral channels, not IQ alone. 
Benin and broader SSA exposure work. These studies are more about prevalence and sources than earnings, but they show why the LMIC problem remains current: many children still have elevated BLLs from ongoing environmental and occupational sources. 
Economic magnitude estimates for LMICs
The large headline numbers for LMIC earnings losses are mostly model-based.
Attina and Trasande’s classic 2013 estimate put the annual economic cost of childhood lead exposure in LMICs at about $977 billion, roughly 1.2% of world GDP at the time. 
Larsen and Sánchez-Triana (2023) updated the global burden using GBD-based BLL estimates and the pooled BLL-IQ function, estimating very large global costs and finding that almost all IQ-loss burden fell in LMICs. 
Ericson and Brown (2025) estimate that for each one-year cohort of 5-year-old children in LMICs, lead-attributable productivity losses are roughly $305–499 billion (2019 USD), or 0.7% to 4.2% of regional GDP, depending on discounting. This is probably the most up-to-date LMIC productivity-loss estimate, but again it is based on modeled IQ decrements translated into earnings losses, not direct observed wages. 
What I would say in one sentence
The literature strongly supports that higher childhood BLL reduces IQ and learning, and that those skill losses plausibly translate into lower adult earnings; however, for LMICs today the earnings claim rests mostly on combining a strong BLL→cognition literature with a good but separate cognition→earnings literature, because direct LMIC causal estimates of BLL→earnings remain rare. 
Two important critiques
First, direct long-run earnings evidence is too sparse, especially in LMICs. The field still leans heavily on indirect valuation: BLL → IQ points lost, then IQ points → lifetime earnings. That may be directionally right while still being wrong in magnitude. 
Second, LMIC lead exposure is often bundled with other insults: arsenic, manganese, air pollution, poor nutrition, anemia, low school quality, and household poverty. Good studies control for many confounders, but residual confounding and effect modification remain real concerns. This matters especially when one tries to convert an observational cognition coefficient into a precise macroeconomic dollar loss. 
Two useful research ideas
One good next paper would be to link child BLL screening data to tax or social-insurance earnings records in an LMIC with administrative depth, ideally using policy or plant-closure shocks for identification.
A second, more original design would combine geocoded lead-source remediation data (battery recycling cleanup, spice enforcement, glaze reform, pipe replacement) with education and labor-market panels, so the field can estimate pathway-specific returns rather than one generic “lead effect.”

The relationship between childhood blood lead levels (BLL) and adult economic outcomes is primarily mediated through irreversible damage to the developing nervous system, which manifests as reduced cognitive ability (IQ) and impaired behavioral regulation.[1, 2] In low- and middle-income countries (LMICs), where lead exposure remains high due to informal industries and contaminated consumer products, these human capital deficits result in staggering macroeconomic losses.
The Pathway from BLL to Earnings
Economic literature typically quantifies the impact of lead by linking three specific variables: blood lead concentration, IQ points, and lifetime earning potential.
 * BLL to IQ: Lead exposure impairs the development of grey matter in the brain.[2] Recent meta-analyses indicate that BLLs of 5 and 15 ug/dL are associated with average decrements of 2.9 and 5.5 IQ points, respectively.[2] The dose-response relationship is non-linear, with the steepest marginal IQ losses occurring at the lowest levels of exposure (below 7.5 ug/dL).
 * IQ to Earnings: Cognitive ability proportionally influences individual earning potential. A loss of a single IQ point is quantified in modern economic models as a 0.5% to 2.5% reduction in lifetime earning capacity. A widely accepted conservative estimate used by public health economists is a 1.4% reduction in market productivity per IQ point lost.
 * Mediating Factors: While IQ is a primary driver, the effect of lead on earnings is also mediated by "soft skills" (impulse control, focus) and educational attainment.[3, 4] For example, higher BLLs increase the probability of dropping out of high school and reliance on public assistance, while decreasing the likelihood of full-time employment.[3]
Evidence from LMICs
Unlike developed nations that rely on historical data (e.g., U.S. Army enlistees or Swedish gasoline phase-outs), recent studies have directly evaluated contemporary LMIC populations.
 * Arica, Chile (Longitudinal Analysis): Researchers analyzed individuals exposed to toxic waste (including lead) and estimated that every extra microgram of lead per deciliter of blood reduces lifetime earnings by approximately $6,000. This equates to a loss of 0.15 standard deviations in math scores and 0.21 standard deviations in language scores per 1 ug/dL increase.[5]
 * India (Health-Productivity Gradient): Research in India has documented a robust negative gradient between early-life disease environments and adult wages. While lead specifically is a major contributor, broader early-life health deficits (proxied by infant mortality) show that a 10-point reduction in local infant mortality is associated with a 2% increase in adult wages. Taller adults in India—a proxy for better early-life nutrition and lower toxin exposure—exhibit a significantly steeper cognitive-achievement slope than those in developed countries.
 * Aggregate Productivity Losses: Recent evaluations by the World Bank and UNICEF estimate that pediatric lead exposure robs LMICs of at least $1 trillion annually in lost economic potential. For a single country like Nepal, annual losses are estimated at $1.5 billion, or roughly 4% of the national GDP.[6]
Macroeconomic Impact in LMICs
Recent studies highlight that the burden of lead exposure is now overwhelmingly borne by LMICs.

The argument for these ranges is as follows.
First, the BLL-to-IQ slope is nonlinear and steeper at lower BLLs, which is why I strongly prefer the log specification over a linear “IQ points per μg/dL” rule. Lanphear’s pooled analysis found a 6.2-point IQ decrement for an increase from <1 to 10 μg/dL, and Canfield found 7.4 points over roughly the same range. Those two studies are still the main empirical anchors for cost-benefit work. 
Second, for contemporary LMICs, it is useful to sanity-check that against the newer developing-country learning meta-analysis: −0.12 SD learning per 1 ln-unit higher BLL. That estimate is not the same outcome as IQ, but it points in the same direction and is arguably more policy-relevant in school-age populations. 
Third, for the cognition-to-earnings step, the cleanest LMIC summary is Ozawa et al.: a 1 SD increase in cognitive skill is associated with about 4.5% higher wages, with a 95% interval of 2.6% to 9.6%. I would use that as the default LMIC earnings mapping. Using larger OECD-style returns such as 18% per SD from PIAAC would usually overstate effects for an LMIC policy appraisal unless you can justify that your setting has labor markets where cognitive skill is strongly rewarded. 
Fourth, there is some direct long-run evidence that lead affects adult socioeconomic outcomes beyond IQ alone. Reuben et al. found that each 5 μg/dL higher childhood BLL was associated with a 1.61-point lower adult IQ and lower adult socioeconomic status, and Banzhaf et al. found that a 0.5 μg/m³ reduction in in utero air lead exposure in the US was associated with about a 3.5% increase in adult earnings. I would use these papers as external validation, not as your main calibration, because one is from New Zealand and the other uses air lead rather than blood lead. But they support the idea that an IQ-only channel may understate total earnings effects because behavior, schooling, disability, and employment also matter.

	Two critiques of this approach.
One, the first leg is still mostly based on observational cohort evidence, albeit very consistent observational evidence. That is good enough for serious policy modeling, but not as clean as a randomized design. 
Two, the second leg maps cognitive test scores into earnings, and those tests are not identical to IQ. Treating 15 IQ points as exactly 1 SD of the wage-relevant skill distribution is a practical approximation, not a theorem. This is why I prefer presenting results as ranges, not point claims. 
Two out-of-the-box improvements that would make your model stronger.
First, run both an IQ channel and a learning channel in parallel, and report the lower of the two as your main estimate. That makes the exercise harder to dismiss as overfit to one literature.
Second, add a separate non-IQ channel sensitivity case for labor-force participation or disability, using the direct long-run lead papers only as a plausibility check. Do not fold that into the main estimate unless you have country-specific justification.

The Grosse and Zhou review synthesizes decades of economic research. Key foundational studies they cite for these proportions include:
 * Schwartz (1994): Estimated a 1.76% increase in annual earnings per IQ point, noting that roughly 72% of this gain is mediated by increased educational attainment.
 * Salkever (1995): Estimated a weighted average of 2.37% (2.09% for males and 3.63% for females) per point of cognitive ability.

Kohl usage 


A consistent body of survey, ethnographic, and clinical evidence suggests that kohl (surma/kajal) use begins very early in life and is widespread across both sexes in infancy, with subsequent gender divergence. Studies from South Asia and North Africa report initiation in the neonatal period or first months of life, often for perceived protective or health benefits rather than cosmetic reasons (Parsons et al. 1997; Shaltout et al. 2011). Cross-sectional surveys in India and Pakistan commonly find that roughly half or more of young children have been exposed, with similar rates for boys and girls in infancy (Khandekar et al. 2007; Rahbar et al. 2002). Clinical case series of lead poisoning linked to kohl reinforce that exposure is concentrated in the first year of life and often repeated, implying that once initiated, use typically persists for months rather than isolated episodes (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention 2012; Al-Ashban et al. 2004). Ethnographic and survey evidence further indicates that use declines with age for boys but persists longer for girls, reflecting a shift from protective to cosmetic motivations (Nriagu et al. 2011; Hardy et al. 2004). Clean one-paragraph (paper-ready, cautious)
Evidence comparing children who use kohl with those who do not—excluding case reports—suggests a consistent but moderate elevation in blood lead levels rather than extreme values. A meta-analysis of 14 studies reports a pooled increase of approximately 5.8 µg/dL associated with kohl use, while individual studies range from roughly 2–3 µg/dL in community samples to 8–9 µg/dL in clinic-based screening populations (Keosaian et al. 2019; Sadeq et al. 2021; CDC screening summaries). Given typical background BLLs of 3–6 µg/dL in comparable settings, these estimates imply total BLLs among exposed children on the order of 8–15 µg/dL. For infants—who may experience somewhat higher absorption but similar exposure patterns—a reasonable working assumption is an incremental effect of approximately 6 µg/dL (plausible range 3–10 µg/dL), corresponding to total BLLs of roughly 10–12 µg/dL in typical exposed populations. These figures should be interpreted as approximate, given heterogeneity in product lead content, frequency of use, and the lack of nationally representative infant-specific data.

│ Survival 18 to 65                           │   0.65  │   0.65  │   0.75  │ World Bank life tables         │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────┼─────────┼─────────┼─────────┼────────────────────────────────┤
│ VSL PARAMETERS (neutral)                                                    │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────┼─────────┼─────────┼─────────┼────────────────────────────────┤
│ Remaining life years at age 18              │   55    │   60    │   65    │ WHO life tables (LMIC)         │
│ VSL multiplier (OECD transfer function)     │   0.30  │  0.404  │   0.50  │ Wijnen et al. 2025             │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────┼─────────┼─────────┼─────────┼────────────────────────────────┤
│ COST PARAMETERS (cost: Max = Mode)                                           │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────┼─────────┼─────────┼─────────┼────────────────────────────────┤
│ Pot alone cost (USD per reached pair)       │   20    │   35    │   35    │ Wolf et al. 2008 + adjustment   │
│ Kohl alone cost (USD per reached pair)      │   10    │   20    │   20    │ Wolf et al. 2008 + adjustment   │
│ Combined cost (USD per reached pair)        │   25    │   45    │   45    │ Wolf et al. 2008 + adjustment   │
│ Discount rate (annual) - treated as cost    │   0.03  │   0.05  │   0.05  │ Neumann et al. 2016             │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────┴─────────┴─────────┴─────────┴────────────────────────────────┘
"""


# ============================================
# PERT FUNCTION (EXTREME CONSERVATIVE)
# ============================================


def pert_rvs_conservative(min_val, mode_val, max_val, size=1, param_type='benefit'):
    """
    Generate PERT-distributed random variates with EXTREME CONSERVATIVE transformation.
    
    For BENEFIT parameters (lower benefit): Use Min = Mode (shifts left)
    For COST parameters (higher cost): Use Max = Mode (shifts right)
    For NEUTRAL parameters: Use original min/mode/max
    """
    
    if param_type == 'benefit':
        # Conservative benefit: mode becomes max (shifts distribution left)
        # This lowers the mean benefit
        min_adj, mode_adj, max_adj = min_val, min_val, mode_val
    elif param_type == 'cost':
        # Conservative cost: mode becomes min (shifts distribution right)
        # This raises the mean cost
        min_adj, mode_adj, max_adj = mode_val, max_val, max_val
    else:
        min_adj, mode_adj, max_adj = min_val, mode_val, max_val
    
    # Standard PERT
    lambda_val = 4
    mean_adj = (min_adj + lambda_val * mode_adj + max_adj) / (lambda_val + 2)
    
    if min_adj == max_adj:
        return np.full(size, min_adj)
    
    scaled_mean = (mean_adj - min_adj) / (max_adj - min_adj)
    nu = (lambda_val + 2) / 2
    alpha = nu * scaled_mean
    beta = nu * (1 - scaled_mean)
    
    beta_samples = stats.beta.rvs(alpha, beta, size=size)
    return min_adj + (max_adj - min_adj) * beta_samples


# ============================================
# GENERATE SHARED PARAMETERS
# ============================================


def generate_shared_parameters(n_samples=50000):
    """Generate parameters that are common across all interventions"""
    
    params = {}
    
    # ===== BLL REDUCTION (100% adherence) - DIRECT INPUT =====
    # These values come from empirical sources and already incorporate
    # daily exposure, lead concentration, transfer coefficients, etc.
    # They represent the expected BLL reduction for a fully adherent user.
    
    params['bll_reduction_pot_100'] = pert_rvs_conservative(5, 7, 10, n_samples, 'benefit')
    params['bll_reduction_kohl_100'] = pert_rvs_conservative(10, 14, 20, n_samples, 'benefit')
    params['bll_reduction_combined_100'] = pert_rvs_conservative(15, 21, 30, n_samples, 'benefit')
    
    # ===== Implementation parameters (benefit: Min = Mode) =====
    params['targeting'] = pert_rvs_conservative(0.20, 0.20, 0.60, n_samples, 'benefit')
    params['delivery'] = pert_rvs_conservative(0.40, 0.40, 0.60, n_samples, 'benefit')
    params['adherence'] = pert_rvs_conservative(0.40, 0.40, 0.60, n_samples, 'benefit')
    
    # ===== Health impact (benefit: Min = Mode) =====
    params['iq_loss_per_bll'] = pert_rvs_conservative(0.18, 0.18, 0.25, n_samples, 'benefit')
    params['daly_weight_child'] = pert_rvs_conservative(0.002, 0.002, 0.005, n_samples, 'benefit')
    params['daly_weight_cvd'] = pert_rvs_conservative(0.10, 0.10, 0.20, n_samples, 'benefit')
    params['cvd_window_years'] = pert_rvs_conservative(15, 15, 20, n_samples, 'benefit')
    
    # ===== Economic parameters (neutral) =====
    params['gdp_per_capita'] = pert_rvs_conservative(3000, 7000, 15000, n_samples, 'neutral')
    params['lfp_rate'] = pert_rvs_conservative(0.50, 0.65, 0.80, n_samples, 'neutral')
    params['earnings_elasticity'] = pert_rvs_conservative(0.005, 0.015, 0.030, n_samples, 'neutral')
    params['working_years'] = pert_rvs_conservative(40, 47, 50, n_samples, 'neutral')
    
    # ===== Survival parameters (benefit: Min = Mode) =====
    params['survival_to_5'] = pert_rvs_conservative(0.88, 0.88, 0.95, n_samples, 'benefit')
    params['survival_5_to_18'] = pert_rvs_conservative(0.97, 0.97, 0.985, n_samples, 'benefit')
    params['survival_18_to_65'] = pert_rvs_conservative(0.65, 0.65, 0.75, n_samples, 'benefit')
    
    # ===== VSL parameters (neutral) =====
    params['remaining_life_years'] = pert_rvs_conservative(55, 60, 65, n_samples, 'neutral')
    params['vsl_multiplier'] = pert_rvs_conservative(0.30, 0.404, 0.50, n_samples, 'neutral')
    
    # ===== Cost parameters (cost: Max = Mode) =====
    params['discount_rate'] = pert_rvs_conservative(0.03, 0.05, 0.05, n_samples, 'cost')
    
    return params


# ============================================
# EFFECTIVE REACH CALCULATION
# ============================================


def calculate_effective_reach(params):
    """
    Calculate the proportion of the population that effectively uses the safe product.
    
    Effective reach = Targeting × Delivery × Adherence
    
    This is the fraction of the total population that:
    1. Was using unsafe product before (targeting)
    2. Receives the safe product (delivery)
    3. Actually uses it instead of the old product (adherence)
    """
    
    effective_reach = params['targeting'] * params['delivery'] * params['adherence']
    
    return effective_reach


# ============================================
# POPULATION BLL REDUCTION
# ============================================


def calculate_population_bll_reduction(params, intervention='combined'):
    """
    Calculate population-average BLL reduction accounting for implementation effectiveness.
    
    Population reduction = Individual reduction (100% adherence) × Effective reach
    """
    
    if intervention == 'pot':
        individual_reduction = params['bll_reduction_pot_100']
    elif intervention == 'kohl':
        individual_reduction = params['bll_reduction_kohl_100']
    else:  # combined
        individual_reduction = params['bll_reduction_combined_100']
    
    effective_reach = calculate_effective_reach(params)
    
    population_reduction = individual_reduction * effective_reach
    
    return population_reduction, individual_reduction, effective_reach


# ============================================
# CALCULATE TOTAL BENEFIT (per capita in population)
# ============================================


def calculate_total_benefit(params, population_bll_reduction):
    """Calculate total economic + health benefit (USD) per CAPITA in total population"""
    
    # Survival to working age
    survival_to_18 = params['survival_to_5'] * params['survival_5_to_18']
    survival_to_65 = survival_to_18 * params['survival_18_to_65']
    
    # IQ gains (using population-average BLL reduction)
    iq_gained = population_bll_reduction * params['iq_loss_per_bll']
    
    # Present value of lifetime earnings at birth
    discount_rate = params['discount_rate']
    annual_earnings = params['gdp_per_capita'] * params['lfp_rate']
    working_years = params['working_years']
    
    # PV at age 18 (annuity formula)
    # Handle edge case where discount_rate is zero
    if np.all(discount_rate <= 0):
        pv_at_18 = annual_earnings * working_years
    else:
        # Use element-wise division for arrays
        pv_at_18 = annual_earnings * (1 - (1 + discount_rate) ** (-working_years)) / discount_rate
    
    # Discount back to birth (18 years)
    pv_at_birth = pv_at_18 / ((1 + discount_rate) ** 18)
    
    # Apply survival probability (must survive to working age to earn)
    pv_adjusted = pv_at_birth * survival_to_18
    
    # Economic value of IQ gains
    economic_value = pv_adjusted * params['earnings_elasticity'] * iq_gained
    
    # DALY calculation
    # VSL per DALY using OECD transfer function
    gni_per_capita = params['gdp_per_capita'] * 0.98  # GNI ≈ GDP for most LMICs
    vsl_millions = params['vsl_multiplier'] * ((gni_per_capita / 5726) ** 1.2)
    vsl = vsl_millions * 1_000_000
    vsl_per_daly = vsl / params['remaining_life_years']
    
    # Child DALYs (ages 0-18, discount to midpoint ~9 years)
    discount_child = 1 / ((1 + discount_rate) ** 9)
    # Scale: BLL reduction in μg/dL to DALYs (divided by 100 for scaling factor)
    child_dalys = (population_bll_reduction * params['daly_weight_child'] * 18 / 100) * discount_child
    
    # Adult CVD DALYs (discount to midpoint ~50 years)
    discount_adult = 1 / ((1 + discount_rate) ** 50)
    adult_dalys = (population_bll_reduction * params['daly_weight_cvd'] * params['cvd_window_years'] / 100)
    adult_dalys = adult_dalys * discount_adult * survival_to_65
    
    total_dalys = child_dalys + adult_dalys
    daly_benefit = total_dalys * vsl_per_daly
    
    # Total benefit (per capita in population)
    total_benefit = economic_value + daly_benefit
    
    return total_benefit, economic_value, daly_benefit


# ============================================
# INTERVENTION COSTS (per capita in population)
# ============================================


def get_intervention_cost_per_capita(intervention, effective_reach, n_samples, params):
    """
    Get cost per CAPITA in total population.
    
    Cost per capita = (Cost per reached individual) × (Effective reach)
    Because we only spend money on those who are reached.
    """
    
    # Cost per individual reached (extreme conservative: Max = Mode)
    # Note: These are PERT distributions with min, mode, max
    if intervention == 'pot':
        cost_per_reached = pert_rvs_conservative(20, 35, 35, n_samples, 'cost')
    elif intervention == 'kohl':
        cost_per_reached = pert_rvs_conservative(10, 20, 20, n_samples, 'cost')
    else:  # combined
        cost_per_reached = pert_rvs_conservative(25, 45, 45, n_samples, 'cost')
    
    # Multiply by effective reach to get cost per capita in population
    cost_per_capita = cost_per_reached * effective_reach
    
    return cost_per_capita, cost_per_reached

# RUN SIMULATION FOR ALL INTERVENTIONS
# ============================================


def run_extreme_conservative_simulation(n_samples=100000):
    """
    Run extreme conservative simulation for all three interventions
    Returns benefit-cost ratios and detailed results
    """
    
    print("="*80)
    print("EXTREME CONSERVATIVE SIMULATION (Lower Benefits + Higher Costs)")
    print(f"Samples: {n_samples:,}")
    print("="*80)
    
    # Generate shared parameters once
    params = generate_shared_parameters(n_samples)
    
    # Calculate effective reach (same for all interventions)
    effective_reach = calculate_effective_reach(params)
    
    print("\n" + "-"*80)
    print("IMPLEMENTATION EFFECTIVENESS PARAMETERS (Extreme Conservative)")
    print("-"*80)
    print(f"  Targeting (using unsafe before):     mean = {np.mean(params['targeting']):.1%}  "
          f"(95% CI: {np.percentile(params['targeting'],2.5):.1%}-{np.percentile(params['targeting'],97.5):.1%})")
    print(f"  Delivery (receive safe product):     mean = {np.mean(params['delivery']):.1%}  "
          f"(95% CI: {np.percentile(params['delivery'],2.5):.1%}-{np.percentile(params['delivery'],97.5):.1%})")
    print(f"  Adherence (use safe instead of old): mean = {np.mean(params['adherence']):.1%}  "
          f"(95% CI: {np.percentile(params['adherence'],2.5):.1%}-{np.percentile(params['adherence'],97.5):.1%})")
    print(f"  EFFECTIVE REACH (product):           mean = {np.mean(effective_reach):.1%}  "
          f"(95% CI: {np.percentile(effective_reach,2.5):.1%}-{np.percentile(effective_reach,97.5):.1%})")
    
    print("\n" + "-"*80)
    print("BLL REDUCTION AT 100% ADHERENCE (Direct from empirical sources)")
    print("-"*80)
    print(f"  Pot alone:   mean = {np.mean(params['bll_reduction_pot_100']):.1f} μg/dL  "
          f"(95% CI: {np.percentile(params['bll_reduction_pot_100'],2.5):.1f}-{np.percentile(params['bll_reduction_pot_100'],97.5):.1f})")
    print(f"  Kohl alone:  mean = {np.mean(params['bll_reduction_kohl_100']):.1f} μg/dL  "
          f"(95% CI: {np.percentile(params['bll_reduction_kohl_100'],2.5):.1f}-{np.percentile(params['bll_reduction_kohl_100'],97.5):.1f})")
    print(f"  Combined:    mean = {np.mean(params['bll_reduction_combined_100']):.1f} μg/dL  "
          f"(95% CI: {np.percentile(params['bll_reduction_combined_100'],2.5):.1f}-{np.percentile(params['bll_reduction_combined_100'],97.5):.1f})")
    
    results = {}
    
    for intervention in ['pot', 'kohl', 'combined']:
        print(f"\n--- {intervention.upper()} intervention ---")
        
        # Calculate BLL reduction (population average)
        pop_bll_red, ind_bll_red, eff_reach = calculate_population_bll_reduction(params, intervention)
        
        # Calculate total benefit (per capita in population)
        total_benefit, economic_benefit, daly_benefit = calculate_total_benefit(params, pop_bll_red)
        
        # Get cost per capita (accounts for effective reach)
        cost_per_capita, cost_per_reached = get_intervention_cost_per_capita(intervention, eff_reach, n_samples, params)
        
        # Benefit-Cost Ratio (using population-level costs and benefits)
        bc_ratio = total_benefit / cost_per_capita
        
        # Store results
        results[intervention] = {
            'benefit_per_capita': total_benefit,
            'economic_benefit': economic_benefit,
            'daly_benefit': daly_benefit,
            'cost_per_capita': cost_per_capita,
            'cost_per_reached': cost_per_reached,
            'bc_ratio': bc_ratio,
            'pop_bll_reduction': pop_bll_red,
            'ind_bll_reduction': ind_bll_red,
            'effective_reach': eff_reach
        }
        
        # Summary statistics
        print(f"  Effective reach:                    {np.mean(eff_reach):.1%}")
        print(f"  Individual BLL reduction (μg/dL):   {np.mean(ind_bll_red):.1f}")
        print(f"  Population BLL reduction (μg/dL):   {np.mean(pop_bll_red):.2f}")
        print(f"  Economic benefit per capita:        ${np.mean(economic_benefit):,.0f}")
        print(f"  DALY benefit per capita:            ${np.mean(daly_benefit):,.0f}")
        print(f"  Total benefit per capita (mean):    ${np.mean(total_benefit):,.0f}")
        print(f"  Cost per capita (mean):             ${np.mean(cost_per_capita):,.0f}")
        print(f"  Cost per reached (mean):            ${np.mean(cost_per_reached):,.0f}")
        print(f"  BC Ratio (mean):                    {np.mean(bc_ratio):.2f}")
        print(f"  BC Ratio (median):                  {np.median(bc_ratio):.2f}")
        print(f"  BC Ratio (95% CI):                  ({np.percentile(bc_ratio, 2.5):.2f}, {np.percentile(bc_ratio, 97.5):.2f})")
        print(f"  Probability BC > 1:                 {np.mean(bc_ratio > 1):.1%}")
        print(f"  Probability BC > 2:                 {np.mean(bc_ratio > 2):.1%}")
        print(f"  Probability BC > 3:                 {np.mean(bc_ratio > 3):.1%}")
    
    return results, params

def plot_bc_ratio_distributions(results, save_path='bc_ratio_distributions.png'):
    """Plot histograms of benefit-cost ratios for all three interventions"""
    
    fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(15, 5))
    
    colors = {'pot': '#2ecc71', 'kohl': '#3498db', 'combined': '#e74c3c'}
    intervention_names = {'pot': 'Pot Replacement Only', 'kohl': 'Kohl Replacement Only', 'combined': 'Combined Intervention'}
    
    for i, (intervention, color) in enumerate(colors.items()):
        bc_ratio = results[intervention]['bc_ratio']
        
        # Trim to 99th percentile for better visualization
        upper_lim = np.percentile(bc_ratio, 99)
        bc_trimmed = bc_ratio[bc_ratio <= upper_lim]
        
        axes[i].hist(bc_trimmed, bins=100, color=color, alpha=0.7, edgecolor='black', density=True)
        axes[i].axvline(x=1, color='red', linestyle='--', linewidth=2, label='BC = 1 (Breakeven)')
        axes[i].axvline(x=np.mean(bc_ratio), color='black', linestyle='-', linewidth=2, 
                        label=f'Mean: {np.mean(bc_ratio):.1f}')
        axes[i].axvline(x=np.median(bc_ratio), color='orange', linestyle=':', linewidth=2,
                        label=f'Median: {np.median(bc_ratio):.1f}')
        
        axes[i].set_xlabel('Benefit-Cost Ratio', fontsize=12)
        axes[i].set_ylabel('Density', fontsize=12)
        axes[i].set_title(f'{intervention_names[intervention]}\n'
                         f'Reach: {np.mean(results[intervention]["effective_reach"]):.1%} | BC>1: {np.mean(bc_ratio>1):.1%}', 
                         fontsize=12)
        axes[i].legend(fontsize=9)
        axes[i].set_xlim(0, min(upper_lim, 20))  # Cap at 20 or 99th percentile
        
        # Add annotation
        axes[i].text(0.95, 0.95, 
                    f'P(BC>1) = {np.mean(bc_ratio>1):.1%}\n'
                    f'P(BC>2) = {np.mean(bc_ratio>2):.1%}\n'
                    f'P(BC>3) = {np.mean(bc_ratio>3):.1%}', 
                    transform=axes[i].transAxes, verticalalignment='top', horizontalalignment='right',
                    bbox=dict(boxstyle='round', facecolor='white', alpha=0.8), fontsize=9)
    
    plt.tight_layout()
    plt.savefig(save_path, dpi=300, bbox_inches='tight')
    plt.show()
    print(f"\nPlot saved to {save_path}")


def plot_implementation_sensitivity(results, params, save_path='implementation_sensitivity.png'):
    """Plot how BC ratio varies with implementation parameters"""
    
    fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 2, figsize=(12, 10))
    
    interventions = ['pot', 'kohl', 'combined']
    colors = {'pot': '#2ecc71', 'kohl': '#3498db', 'combined': '#e74c3c'}
    labels = {'pot': 'Pot Only', 'kohl': 'Kohl Only', 'combined': 'Combined'}
    
    # Plot 1: BC Ratio vs Effective Reach
    ax = axes[0, 0]
    for intervention in interventions:
        bc = results[intervention]['bc_ratio']
        reach = results[intervention]['effective_reach']
        # Bin by reach deciles
        reach_deciles = np.percentile(reach, np.linspace(0, 100, 11))
        bc_means = []
        reach_means = []
        for i in range(len(reach_deciles)-1):
            mask = (reach >= reach_deciles[i]) & (reach < reach_deciles[i+1])
            if np.sum(mask) > 0:
                bc_means.append(np.mean(bc[mask]))
                reach_means.append(np.mean(reach[mask]))
        ax.plot(reach_means, bc_means, 'o-', color=colors[intervention], label=labels[intervention])
    ax.set_xlabel('Effective Reach', fontsize=12)
    ax.set_ylabel('Mean Benefit-Cost Ratio', fontsize=12)
    ax.set_title('BC Ratio vs Implementation Effectiveness', fontsize=12)
    ax.axhline(y=1, color='red', linestyle='--', alpha=0.5)
    ax.legend()
    ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
    
    # Plot 2: Distribution of effective reach
    ax = axes[0, 1]
    for intervention in interventions:
        reach = results[intervention]['effective_reach']
        ax.hist(reach, bins=50, alpha=0.5, color=colors[intervention], label=labels[intervention], density=True)
    ax.set_xlabel('Effective Reach', fontsize=12)
    ax.set_ylabel('Density', fontsize=12)
    ax.set_title('Distribution of Effective Reach', fontsize=12)
    ax.legend()
    ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
    
    # Plot 3: BC Ratio distribution (boxplot)
    ax = axes[1, 0]
    data = [results['pot']['bc_ratio'], results['kohl']['bc_ratio'], results['combined']['bc_ratio']]
    bp = ax.boxplot(data, labels=['Pot Only', 'Kohl Only', 'Combined'], patch_artist=True, showfliers=False)
    for patch, color in zip(bp['boxes'], [colors['pot'], colors['kohl'], colors['combined']]):
        patch.set_facecolor(color)
        patch.set_alpha(0.7)
    ax.axhline(y=1, color='red', linestyle='--', linewidth=2, label='Breakeven')
    ax.set_ylabel('Benefit-Cost Ratio', fontsize=12)
    ax.set_title('BC Ratio Comparison Across Interventions', fontsize=12)
    ax.set_yscale('log')
    ax.legend()
    ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
    
    # Plot 4: Decomposition of effective reach (average values)
    ax = axes[1, 1]
    components = ['Targeting', 'Delivery', 'Adherence', 'Effective\nReach']
    mean_vals = [np.mean(params['targeting']), np.mean(params['delivery']), 
                 np.mean(params['adherence']), np.mean(results['combined']['effective_reach'])]
    ci_low = [np.percentile(params['targeting'], 2.5), np.percentile(params['delivery'], 2.5),
              np.percentile(params['adherence'], 2.5), np.percentile(results['combined']['effective_reach'], 2.5)]
    ci_high = [np.percentile(params['targeting'], 97.5), np.percentile(params['delivery'], 97.5),
               np.percentile(params['adherence'], 97.5), np.percentile(results['combined']['effective_reach'], 97.5)]
    
    x_pos = np.arange(len(components))
    ax.bar(x_pos, mean_vals, color=['#2ecc71', '#3498db', '#e67e22', '#e74c3c'], alpha=0.7)
    ax.errorbar(x_pos, mean_vals, yerr=[mean_vals[i] - ci_low[i] for i in range(len(mean_vals))], 
                fmt='none', color='black', capsize=5)
    ax.set_ylabel('Proportion', fontsize=12)
    ax.set_title('Decomposition of Effective Reach (Combined Intervention)', fontsize=12)
    ax.set_xticks(x_pos)
    ax.set_xticklabels(components)
    ax.set_ylim(0, 1)
    ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3, axis='y')
    
    plt.tight_layout()
    plt.savefig(save_path, dpi=300, bbox_inches='tight')
    plt.show()
    print(f"Plot saved to {save_path}")

df_params = pd.read_csv(io.StringIO(CSV_DATA), comment="#").set_index("Variable")
np.random.seed(42)

# Explicit duration assumptions for extension path
YEARS_POT = 4.0
YEARS_CALCIUM = 1.0
YEARS_KOHL_MATERNAL = 1.0
YEARS_KOHL_INFANT = 1.0  # child benefit only, not adult CVD
YEARS_MUG = 2.0  # child benefit only, not adult CVD




def draw_pert(var_name, size=N_TRIALS, is_conservative=False, alpha=0.75):
    row = df_params.loc[var_name]
    min_v, mode_v, max_v = float(row["Min"]), float(row["Mode"]), float(row["Max"])
    if is_conservative:
        is_cost = str(row["Is_Cost"]).strip().lower() == "true"
        if is_cost:
            min_v = alpha * min_v + (1 - alpha) * mode_v
            mode_v = alpha * mode_v + (1 - alpha) * max_v
        else:
            max_v = alpha * max_v + (1 - alpha) * mode_v
            mode_v = alpha * mode_v + (1 - alpha) * min_v
    r = max_v - min_v
    if r == 0:
        return np.full(size, mode_v)
    a = 1 + 4 * (mode_v - min_v) / r
    b = 1 + 4 * (max_v - mode_v) / r
    return stats.beta.rvs(a, b, size=size) * r + min_v




def induce_correlation(arrays, corr_val):
    size = len(arrays[0])
    z = np.random.normal(size=(2, size))
    L = cholesky([[1.0, corr_val], [corr_val, 1.0]], lower=True)
    z_corr = L @ z
    ranks = np.argsort(np.argsort(z_corr, axis=1), axis=1)
    return [np.sort(arrays[i])[ranks[i]] for i in range(2)]




def calculate_pv_earnings(productivity, growth, discount, p18, p65, k_mort, labor_entry_age):
    """PV of lifetime earnings from birth using unconditional survival over ages 18-65 inclusive."""
    x = (1 + growth) / (1 + discount)
    # Ages 18..65 inclusive => 48 working years when entry age = 18.
    t_work = np.round(65 - labor_entry_age + 1).astype(int)
    t_idx = np.arange(1, 53).reshape(-1, 1)  # max 52 years if labor entry age = 14


    # Unconditional survival from birth to working year t.
    # Use denominator 47 so entry at t=1 maps to age 18 and t=48 maps to age 65.
    st = p18 - (p18 - p65) * (np.maximum(0, t_idx - 1) / 47.0) ** k_mort
    pv_sum = np.sum((t_idx <= t_work) * st * x ** (t_idx - 1), axis=0)
    return productivity * (x ** labor_entry_age) * pv_sum




def run_simulation(is_conservative=False):
    p = {var: draw_pert(var, is_conservative=is_conservative) for var in df_params.index}
    p["p_fid_clinical"], p["p_adh_pills"] = induce_correlation(
        [p["p_fid_clinical"], p["p_adh_pills"]], 0.5
    )


    # Computed productivity
    productivity = p["gdp_ppp_per_capita"] * p["lfp"]


    # ---------------------------------------------------------
    # 1. SOURCE-SPECIFIC BLL PATHWAYS (Reality Chain)
    # ---------------------------------------------------------
    # Pot (shared household)
    p_success_pot = (
        p["p_att_anc"]
        * p["p_fid_voucher"]
        * p["p_redemption"]
        * p["p_adh_hardware"]
        * p["p_prevalence"]
    )
    eff_bll_pot = p["bll_pot"] * p_success_pot


    # Calcium (maternal personal)
    p_success_calc = p["p_att_anc"] * p["p_fid_clinical"] * p["p_adh_pills"] * p["p_prevalence"]
    eff_bll_calc = p["bll_calc"] * p_success_calc


    # Mug (child personal, clinic-delivered)
    p_success_mug = p["p_att_imm"] * p["p_fid_clinical"] * p["p_adh_hardware"] * p["p_prevalence"]
    eff_bll_mug = p["bll_mug"] * p_success_mug


    # Maternal kohl (ANC-delivered)
    p_success_kohl_mom = p_success_calc
    eff_bll_kohl_mom = p["bll_maternal"] * p_success_kohl_mom


    # Infant kohl (facility birth-delivered, baseline prevalence fixed at 1.0)
    is_daughter = np.random.binomial(1, 0.5, N_TRIALS)
    p_success_kohl_inf = p["p_att_birth"] * p["p_fid_clinical"] * p["p_adh_hardware"]
    eff_bll_kohl_inf = (is_daughter * p["bll_infant"]) * p_success_kohl_inf


    # Child cognitive pathway: include child-relevant components only.
    total_eff_bll_child = eff_bll_pot + eff_bll_calc + eff_bll_mug + eff_bll_kohl_inf
    delta_iq = total_eff_bll_child * p["iq_per_bll"]


    # ---------------------------------------------------------
    # 2. COSTING (Conditioned on Attendance/Fidelity/Redemption)
    # ---------------------------------------------------------
    cost = p["fixed_surveillance"]


    # Pot costs: contact at ANC; variable cost only with voucher availability; product cost only when redeemed.
    cost += p["c_explain"] * p["p_att_anc"]
    cost += p["c_dist_bulk"] * 1.1 * p["p_att_anc"] * p["p_fid_voucher"]
    cost += p["c_prod_pot"] * (1 + p["markup_pot"]) * 1.1 * p["p_att_anc"] * p["p_fid_voucher"] * p["p_redemption"]


    # Calcium costs
    cost += p["c_explain"] * p["p_att_anc"]
    cost += p["c_dist_light"] * 1.1 * p["p_att_anc"] * p["p_fid_clinical"]
    cost += p["c_prod_calc"] * 1.1 * p["p_att_anc"] * p["p_fid_clinical"]


    # Mug costs
    cost += p["c_explain"] * p["p_att_imm"]
    cost += p["c_dist_light"] * 1.1 * p["p_att_imm"] * p["p_fid_clinical"]
    cost += p["c_prod_mug"] * 1.1 * p["p_att_imm"] * p["p_fid_clinical"]


    # Maternal kohl costs
    cost += p["c_explain"] * p["p_att_anc"]
    cost += p["c_dist_light"] * 1.1 * p["p_att_anc"] * p["p_fid_clinical"]
    cost += p["c_prod_kohl_maternal"] * 1.1 * p["p_att_anc"] * p["p_fid_clinical"]


    # Infant kohl costs: only daughters receive infant product.
    cost += p["c_explain"] * p["p_att_birth"]
    cost += p["c_dist_light"] * 1.1 * p["p_att_birth"] * p["p_fid_clinical"]
    cost += is_daughter * p["c_prod_kohl_infant"] * 1.1 * p["p_att_birth"] * p["p_fid_clinical"]


    # ---------------------------------------------------------
    # 3. EARNINGS PATHWAY
    # ---------------------------------------------------------
    pv_earnings = calculate_pv_earnings(
        productivity,
        p["growth"],
        p["discount"],
        p["p18"],
        p["p65"],
        p["k_mort"],
        p["labor_entry_age"],
    )
    ben_earnings = pv_earnings * p["earn_per_iq"] * delta_iq


    # ---------------------------------------------------------
    # 4. CVD GENERATIONAL BENEFITS (SUPPLEMENTAL)
    # ---------------------------------------------------------
    vsl_daly = p["vsly_mult"] * (productivity / p["labor_share"])


    # Grandparent benefit: pot only, 4-year duration, half the pot effect, weighted by coresidence.
    ben_cvd_gp = (
        eff_bll_pot
        * p["cvd_daly_per_ug"]
        * YEARS_POT
        * vsl_daly
        * 0.5
        * (1 / (1 + p["discount"]) ** np.maximum(0, 65 - p["gp_age"]))
        * p["gp_coresidence_prob"]
    )


    # Mother benefit: pot half-effect for one of two adults, plus calcium and maternal kohl.
    ben_cvd_mom = (
        ((eff_bll_pot * YEARS_POT * 0.5) + (eff_bll_calc * YEARS_CALCIUM) + (eff_bll_kohl_mom * YEARS_KOHL_MATERNAL))
        * p["cvd_daly_per_ug"]
        * vsl_daly
        * (1 / (1 + p["discount"]) ** np.maximum(0, 65 - p["mom_age"]))
    )


    return pd.DataFrame(
        {
            "BCR_Cog": ben_earnings / cost,
            "BCR_Total": (ben_earnings + ben_cvd_mom + ben_cvd_gp) / cost,
            "Cost": cost,
            "PV_Earnings": pv_earnings,
            "Eff_BLL_Child": total_eff_bll_child,
            "Eff_BLL_Pot": eff_bll_pot,
            "Eff_BLL_Calc": eff_bll_calc,
            "Eff_BLL_Mug": eff_bll_mug,
            "Eff_BLL_Kohl_Mom": eff_bll_kohl_mom,
            "Eff_BLL_Kohl_Inf": eff_bll_kohl_inf,
        }
    )




if __name__ == "__main__":
    res_base = run_simulation(False)

While the primary economic justification for lead reduction rests on aggregate productivity gains across the entire population, the impact on the "left tail" of the IQ distribution provides a distinct and clinically significant metric of success. Using a standard normal distribution model (\mu = 100, \sigma = 15), a 1-point reduction in mean population IQ increases the prevalence of Intellectual Disability (ID, IQ < 70) by approximately 0.38 percentage points, and Severe Intellectual Disability (IQ < 50) by 0.02 percentage points (Fewtrell et al., 2004).
Applying the Global Burden of Disease (GBD) disability weight for mild ID (DW = 0.043) and discounting lifetime disability over a 70-year life expectancy at 3%, a 1-point IQ gain avoids approximately 0.0048 DALYs per child. Valuing a DALY at a conservative 3x GDP per capita for a lower-middle-income country (~$17,400 PPP) translates to a social value of approximately $83 per child. While this figure is significantly lower than the total estimated economic loss—which captures the 1% earnings decline for the 97% of children who remain above the disability threshold—it independently justifies the intervention. Even under this restrictive "threshold-only" model, a lead-safe pot costing $30 yields a nearly 3-to-1 benefit-cost ratio on the basis of avoided clinical disability alone.
